Your uncle's kirana shop buys a delivery van. The van works for years, slowly wearing out.
Depreciation is how the books show that slow wearing out — a little cost every year, instead of
one big cost in the year of purchase. This chapter teaches you four ways to calculate it, what to
do when an asset is sold, and what to do when your first estimate turns out wrong.

In the exam this chapter is dependable: expect at least one full numerical (calculate depreciation,
or profit/loss on sale) plus quick conceptual questions. The numericals follow fixed patterns —
learn the four patterns in the worked examples below and there are no surprises left.

## 1. What depreciation really is

Depreciation is **spreading the cost of an asset over the years it is used**. It is an
*allocation* of cost, not a *valuation*. The books do not try to show what the van would sell
for today — they only spread its cost fairly over its working life.

Why spread it? The **matching idea**: the van helps the shop earn money for (say) six years, so
its cost should sit against six years of income — not crush the profit of year one alone.

Three causes make assets lose usefulness:

- **Wear and tear** — machines and vehicles wear out with use.
- **Passage of time** — some rights simply expire. A 10-year licence loses value each year even
  if it is never used.
- **Obsolescence** — a newer, better technology makes the old one uneconomic. A perfectly working
  billing machine can still be obsolete.

<Callout kind="pointer">
  <p>
    Depreciation is charged <strong>even in a loss year, and even if the asset was idle</strong>.
    It is not optional and does not depend on profit. Also note: depreciation itself involves
    <strong>no cash payment</strong> — the cash went out when the asset was bought.
  </p>
</Callout>

**One word, three cousins.** The same spreading idea has three names depending on the asset:
**depreciation** for tangible assets (machines, vans), **amortisation** for intangible assets
(patents, licences, software), and **depletion** for natural resources (a coal mine gives up
its coal). **Land is not depreciated** — its useful life is unlimited.

## 2. The five terms every question uses

1. **Cost** — everything spent to bring the asset to its place and working condition: purchase
   price + freight + installation + trial runs, minus any trade discount. (Same idea you met in
   capital expenditure.)
2. **Residual value** (scrap value) — what you expect to recover when you dispose of the asset at
   the end.
3. **Depreciable amount** = Cost − Residual value. **This is what gets spread.**
4. **Useful life** — the years (or units of output) the business expects to use the asset. It is
   an estimate, not a fact from a table.
5. **Carrying amount / Written-down value (WDV)** = Cost − depreciation charged so far. This is
   the figure standing in the books today.

Quick check: a machine costs ₹1,10,000, expected scrap ₹10,000. The depreciable amount is
₹1,00,000 — you never depreciate the ₹10,000 you expect to get back.

<Callout kind="mistake">
  <p>
    The single most common slip in this chapter: using <strong>cost</strong> where the formula
    needs <strong>cost − residual value</strong>. Any option in an MCQ that equals
    “cost ÷ life” exactly is usually this trap, planted for you.
  </p>
</Callout>

## 3. Straight Line Method (SLM)

The same amount every year:

<div class="formula">SLM depreciation per year = (Cost − Residual value) ÷ Useful life</div>

The rate, when asked as a percentage, is on the **original cost** and stays fixed.

Mini example: Sharma &amp; Sons (a GST-registered wholesaler in Indore) buys a packing machine for
₹2,60,000, expects to use it 5 years and then sell it for ₹10,000. Yearly charge =
(2,60,000 − 10,000) ÷ 5 = **₹50,000**, every year, for five years. After 5 years its carrying
amount equals exactly the residual value, ₹10,000. SLM always lands exactly on residual value —
that is a good self-check in the exam.

## 4. Written Down Value method (WDV)

A **fixed percentage** is applied every year to the **opening carrying amount** — not to cost.
So the charge is big in early years and keeps shrinking.

Mini example at 20% WDV on a ₹2,00,000 machine:

| Year | Opening WDV | Depreciation @20% | Closing WDV |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 2,00,000 | 40,000 | 1,60,000 |
| 2 | 1,60,000 | 32,000 | 1,28,000 |
| 3 | 1,28,000 | 25,600 | 1,02,400 |

Notice the charge falls every year (40,000 → 32,000 → 25,600). WDV never reaches zero by itself —
it keeps shrinking toward the residual value.

When a question asks you to **find the WDV rate** that reaches a given residual value in *n*
years:

<div class="formula">Rate = 1 − (Residual value ÷ Cost)^(1/n)</div>

Friendly numbers are always chosen. Cost ₹1,00,000, residual ₹12,500, life 3 years:
12,500 ÷ 1,00,000 = 0.125, and the cube root of 0.125 is 0.5. Rate = 1 − 0.5 = **50%**.

**Which method for what?** SLM suits assets that give equal service every year (furniture,
buildings). WDV suits assets that lose more value early and need rising repair costs later
(vehicles, machinery) — the falling depreciation plus rising repairs keeps the total yearly
burden roughly even.

## 5. Units of Production method

When usage, not time, wears the asset out, charge by output:

<div class="formula">Charge for the year = Depreciable amount × (Units produced this year ÷ Total expected lifetime units)</div>

Mini example: a bottling machine costs ₹3,50,000, residual ₹50,000, expected lifetime output
1,50,000 crates. This year it filled 30,000 crates. Charge = 3,00,000 × 30,000/1,50,000 =
**₹60,000**.

## 6. Assets bought or sold mid-year (pro-rata)

Depreciation is charged **from the date the asset is ready for use, to the date of disposal** —
month-wise in Foundation problems. A machine bought on 1 October gets only 6 months' depreciation
in a March-ending year. Unless the question says otherwise, do not charge a full year on
mid-year additions.

## 7. Selling an asset: profit or loss on disposal

Three steps, always the same:

1. Charge depreciation **up to the date of sale** (pro-rata).
2. Find the WDV on the date of sale.
3. Compare: <strong>Sale price − WDV</strong>. Positive = profit on sale; negative = loss on sale.

The profit or loss goes to the Profit &amp; Loss Account. It is compared with **WDV, never with
original cost** — an asset sold below cost but above WDV gives a *profit*.

## 8. When your estimate was wrong: revisions

Useful life and residual value are estimates. When facts change, the estimate changes — and the
fix is always **prospective**: past years are *not* reopened. You take the carrying amount as it
stands, and spread what is left over the *remaining* revised life:

<div class="formula">New yearly charge = (Carrying amount − Revised residual value) ÷ Remaining revised life</div>

The same treatment applies to a **change of method** (say SLM to WDV): under AS 10 it is treated
as a change in accounting *estimate* — apply the new method to the carrying amount from now on.
No retrospective recalculation, no "surplus written back" drama. (Older books show a
retrospective treatment — that belongs to the pre-revision AS 6 era. Follow the current SM.)

## 9. Two ways to record it

- **Direct method:** credit the Asset account each year (Depreciation A/c Dr → To Asset A/c).
  The asset appears at WDV in the balance sheet.
- **Provision for Depreciation method:** the Asset account stays at original cost; each year's
  charge accumulates in a separate **Provision for Depreciation** (accumulated depreciation)
  account. On sale, both the cost and the accumulated provision for that asset are transferred
  out to an Asset Disposal account.

Either way, the Profit &amp; Loss Account is debited with the same yearly charge.

## Fully worked examples

<Callout kind="example" label="Worked example 1 — SLM with a cost build-up (easy)">
  <p>
    On 1 April 2025 Sharma &amp; Sons buys a delivery van: price ₹4,60,000, RTO registration and
    insurance-at-purchase ₹25,000, seller's trade discount ₹10,000, van body painting with shop
    branding ₹25,000. Expected life 6 years, expected scrap ₹50,000. Find the yearly SLM charge.
  </p>
  <div class="working-note">
    <div class="working-note-label">Working note 1 — Cost of the van</div>
    4,60,000 − 10,000 (trade discount) + 25,000 (registration) + 25,000 (branding) = <strong>₹5,00,000</strong>
  </div>
  <div class="working-note">
    <div class="working-note-label">Working note 2 — Yearly charge</div>
    Depreciable amount = 5,00,000 − 50,000 = 4,50,000<br />
    SLM charge = 4,50,000 ÷ 6 = <strong>₹75,000 per year</strong>
  </div>
</Callout>

<Callout kind="example" label="Worked example 2 — WDV, then a sale (moderate)">
  <p>
    Meena Traders buys a machine on 1 April 2023 for ₹4,00,000 and depreciates it at 25% WDV
    (year ends 31 March). On 1 April 2025 the machine is sold for ₹2,00,000. Profit or loss?
  </p>
  <div class="working-note">
    <div class="working-note-label">Working note 1 — WDV on date of sale</div>
    2023–24: 4,00,000 × 25% = 1,00,000 → WDV 3,00,000<br />
    2024–25: 3,00,000 × 25% = 75,000 → WDV <strong>2,25,000</strong><br />
    (Sold on 1 April 2025 — no depreciation for 2025–26.)
  </div>
  <div class="working-note">
    <div class="working-note-label">Working note 2 — Result of sale</div>
    Sale price 2,00,000 − WDV 2,25,000 = <strong>Loss ₹25,000</strong> (to P&amp;L A/c)
  </div>
</Callout>

<Callout kind="example" label="Worked example 3 — mid-year sale with pro-rata depreciation (moderate-hard)">
  <p>
    Verma Industries buys machinery for ₹3,00,000 on 1 April 2023; SLM at 10% p.a. on cost;
    books close 31 March. The machinery is sold on 1 October 2025 for ₹2,05,000. Find the profit
    or loss on sale.
  </p>
  <div class="working-note">
    <div class="working-note-label">Working note 1 — Depreciation charged</div>
    2023–24 (full year): 30,000<br />
    2024–25 (full year): 30,000<br />
    2025–26 (1 Apr – 1 Oct = 6 months): 30,000 × 6/12 = 15,000<br />
    Total depreciation = <strong>₹75,000</strong>
  </div>
  <div class="working-note">
    <div class="working-note-label">Working note 2 — WDV and result</div>
    WDV on 1 Oct 2025 = 3,00,000 − 75,000 = 2,25,000<br />
    Sale 2,05,000 − WDV 2,25,000 = <strong>Loss ₹20,000</strong>
  </div>
</Callout>

<Callout kind="example" label="Worked example 4 — revision of useful life (hard: the examiner's favourite)">
  <p>
    Plant cost ₹8,00,000 on 1 April 2022; residual value ₹80,000; life 8 years; SLM. At the start
    of year 4 (1 April 2025) the total life is revised to 5 years and residual value to ₹30,000.
    Depreciation for 2025–26?
  </p>
  <div class="working-note">
    <div class="working-note-label">Working note 1 — Position after 3 years</div>
    Old charge = (8,00,000 − 80,000) ÷ 8 = 90,000 per year<br />
    Charged for 3 years = 2,70,000 → Carrying amount = 8,00,000 − 2,70,000 = <strong>5,30,000</strong>
  </div>
  <div class="working-note">
    <div class="working-note-label">Working note 2 — Spread what is left, prospectively</div>
    Remaining life = 5 − 3 = 2 years<br />
    New charge = (5,30,000 − 30,000) ÷ 2 = <strong>₹2,50,000 per year</strong><br />
    Past years are not touched — no catch-up entry.
  </div>
</Callout>

## Common mistakes &amp; examiner traps

- **Cost vs depreciable amount.** Using cost ÷ life when a residual value is given. The wrong
  option computed this way is nearly always present.
- **WDV applied to cost.** Applying the WDV rate to original cost every year (that's SLM
  behaviour). The rate applies to the *opening carrying amount*.
- **Ignoring pro-rata months.** A 1 July purchase gets 9 months' charge in a March-ending year.
  Wrong options show the 12-month figure.
- **Profit on sale measured against cost.** Compare sale price with **WDV**. Selling below cost
  but above WDV is a profit.
- **Retrospective revision.** After a life/residual revision (or method change), never recompute
  old years. Spread the current carrying amount forward. An option showing a "catch-up"
  adjustment is the trap.
- **Depreciating land**, or skipping depreciation because the asset was idle or the year made a
  loss — all three are wrong.
- **Forgetting the residual value changes too.** In revision questions, read whether the residual
  value was also revised (Worked example 4: it fell to ₹30,000). Using the old residual is the
  most common slip in the hardest variant.

## One-page revision summary

**Terms:** Depreciable amount = Cost − Residual value · WDV (carrying amount) = Cost −
depreciation to date · Land: never depreciated · Intangibles: amortisation (AS 26) · Natural
resources: depletion.

**Formulas:**

<div class="formula">SLM yearly charge = (Cost − Residual) ÷ Life</div>
<div class="formula">WDV yearly charge = Rate % × Opening carrying amount</div>
<div class="formula">WDV rate to hit residual in n years = 1 − (Residual ÷ Cost)^(1/n)</div>
<div class="formula">Units method = (Cost − Residual) × Year's units ÷ Lifetime units</div>
<div class="formula">Revision (prospective) = (Carrying amount − Revised residual) ÷ Remaining revised life</div>
<div class="formula">Profit/(Loss) on sale = Sale price − WDV on date of sale</div>

**Rules that answer theory MCQs:** allocation not valuation · charged even in loss years and on
idle assets · non-cash charge · pro-rata from ready-for-use date to disposal date · change of
method or estimate = prospective (AS 10) · SLM ends exactly at residual; WDV never reaches zero ·
Provision-for-Depreciation method keeps the asset at cost; disposal goes through an Asset
Disposal account · profit/loss on sale = against WDV, to P&amp;L.
