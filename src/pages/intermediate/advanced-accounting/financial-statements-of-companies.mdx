Every other chapter in this paper eventually lands here: whatever you account for —
buybacks, amalgamations, branches — must finally be *presented* in one prescribed shape.
This chapter is that shape. Unit 1 gives the statutory frame (who keeps which books, what
"financial statements" legally means) and the Schedule III Division I formats with their
current/non-current logic. Unit 2 adds the statement examiners love most: the AS 3 cash
flow statement, where every rupee of movement must be filed under operating, investing or
financing.

## 1. The statutory frame

**Books of account (s.128).** Every company keeps books of account, other relevant books
and financial statements at its **registered office** — a different place in India is
allowed if the Board decides and the Registrar is notified. Books may be kept in
**electronic mode**. They must give a true and fair view, be kept on **accrual basis and
double-entry**, and be preserved for **at least eight financial years** (longer if an
investigation has been ordered).

**Financial statements (s.2(40))** are a defined set: balance sheet, statement of profit
and loss, **cash flow statement**, statement of changes in equity (where applicable), and
the explanatory notes. One carve-out recurs in exams: a **One Person Company, small
company and dormant company** may skip the cash flow statement.

**Final accounts (s.129).** Financial statements must give a **true and fair view**,
comply with the accounting standards, and follow **Schedule III**. Division I of the
Schedule serves companies under the Companies (Accounting Standards) Rules; Division II
re-casts it for Ind AS companies. This chapter lives in **Division I**.

## 2. The Schedule III balance sheet

Division I prescribes a **vertical** balance sheet: *Equity and Liabilities* on top,
*Assets* below, everything split between current and non-current.

| Equity and Liabilities | Assets |
|---|---|
| Shareholders' funds (share capital, reserves & surplus, money against warrants) | Non-current assets (PPE & intangibles, non-current investments, deferred tax asset, long-term loans & advances, other) |
| Share application money pending allotment | Current assets (current investments, inventories, trade receivables, cash & equivalents, short-term loans & advances, other) |
| Non-current liabilities (long-term borrowings, deferred tax liability, other, long-term provisions) | |
| Current liabilities (short-term borrowings, trade payables, other current liabilities, short-term provisions) | |

**Share application money pending allotment** sits on its own line between shareholders'
funds and non-current liabilities — but only to the extent it is **not refundable**; any
amount due for refund (or where allotment is overdue) belongs in **other current
liabilities**.

The engine underneath the format is the **operating cycle**: the time between acquiring
assets for processing and their realisation in cash or cash equivalents. Where the cycle
cannot be identified, it is **assumed to be twelve months**.

## 3. The classification tests

An asset is **current** if it satisfies any one of: expected to be realised in (or
intended for sale/consumption in) the **normal operating cycle**; held primarily for
**trading**; expected to be realised within **twelve months** of the reporting date; or
it is **cash or a cash equivalent** (unless restricted beyond twelve months). All other
assets are non-current.

A liability is **current** if any one holds: expected to be settled in the normal
operating cycle; held primarily for trading; due to be settled within **twelve months**;
or the company **lacks an unconditional right to defer settlement beyond twelve months**.

<Callout kind="pointer">
  <p>
    <strong>The operating cycle can rescue an item past twelve months.</strong> A
    shipbuilder whose cycle runs fifteen months classifies inventory realisable in
    fourteen months as CURRENT — the cycle test and the twelve-month test are
    alternatives, and satisfying either one is enough. The reverse trap: the portion of a
    term loan instalment falling due within twelve months is always a current liability
    ("current maturities of long-term debt"), however long the loan's tail.
  </p>
</Callout>

### Worked example 1 — classification with a long cycle

Narmada Shipworks holds raw material 4 months, works on hulls 3 months, holds finished
craft 5 months and collects from customers in 3 months: operating cycle = 4 + 3 + 5 + 3 =
**15 months**. Inventory expected to convert in 14 months → **current** (within the
cycle). A ₹12,00,000 term loan repays in six half-yearly instalments of ₹2,00,000, the
first due 6 months from the balance sheet date → instalments due within twelve months =
2 × ₹2,00,000 = **₹4,00,000 current**, ₹8,00,000 non-current. A security deposit with the
electricity board refundable after three years → **non-current**, whatever the cycle.

## 4. The disclosures examiners test

- **Share capital note**: authorised, issued, subscribed and paid-up; a **reconciliation
  of the number of shares outstanding** at the beginning and end of the year; rights and
  restrictions per class; shares held by the holding company; **shareholders holding more
  than 5%**, with the number of shares; shares reserved under options.
- **Reserves and surplus**: itemised (capital reserve, securities premium, revaluation
  reserve, general reserve, surplus…), with additions and deductions shown. A **debit
  balance of the statement of profit and loss** is shown as a *negative* figure under
  "Surplus" — even if the net result of reserves and surplus is negative.
- **Contingent liabilities and commitments** appear in the **notes**, never on the face:
  claims not acknowledged as debts, guarantees, estimated value of unexecuted capital
  contracts.
- **Rounding off** (mandatory): total income below ₹100 crore → round to the nearest
  hundreds, thousands, lakhs or millions (or decimals thereof); total income of ₹100
  crore or more → nearest **lakhs, millions or crores** (or decimals thereof).
- **Dividends proposed after the year end** are NOT a liability (AS 4 as amended) — they
  are disclosed in the notes and recognised only when declared.

### Worked example 2 — the share capital reconciliation

Chenab Textiles began the year with 8,00,000 equity shares of ₹10. Mid-year it made a
1-for-4 rights issue, fully subscribed: 8,00,000 ÷ 4 = 2,00,000 new shares. Closing
position: **10,00,000 shares, ₹1,00,00,000 paid-up capital** — and the note must show the
opening count, the movement and the closing count, not just the closing rupees.

## 5. The statement of profit and loss

Division I classifies expenses **by nature** — revenue from operations, other income,
then cost of materials consumed, purchases of stock-in-trade, changes in inventories,
employee benefits expense, finance costs, depreciation and amortisation, other expenses.
No cost-of-sales/administration/selling split (that is classification by function).
Revenue from operations excludes amounts collected as GST — a tax collected for
government is not the company's revenue. Exceptional and extraordinary items (AS 5
survives in Division I) get their own lines before tax.

## 6. Cash flow statements: the AS 3 frame

The cash flow statement explains the movement in **cash and cash equivalents**. Cash
equivalents are **short-term, highly liquid investments readily convertible into known
amounts of cash, subject to insignificant risk of change in value** — as a working rule,
investments with a maturity of **three months or less from the date of acquisition**. A
six-month fixed deposit fails however close to maturity it now is; the test looks at the
term when you acquired it. Equity shares are out (value risk), except something like
redeemable preference shares bought weeks before their fixed redemption.

Every other movement is filed under one of three activities:

- **Operating** — the principal revenue-producing activities: cash from customers,
  payments to suppliers and employees, **taxes on income unless specifically
  attributable** to investing or financing.
- **Investing** — acquisition and disposal of long-term assets and investments: buying
  and selling PPE, loans to third parties, and (for a non-financial company) **interest
  and dividends received**.
- **Financing** — changes in the size and composition of owners' capital and borrowings:
  share issues, buybacks, raising and repaying loans, and (non-financial company)
  **interest and dividends paid**. AS 3 treats **bank overdrafts and cash credits as
  borrowings — financing activity**, not as negative cash.

<Callout kind="mistake">
  <p>
    <strong>Interest and dividends flip with the business.</strong> For a
    <em>financial</em> enterprise, interest paid and interest/dividends received are
    OPERATING flows — lending is its revenue-producing activity. For everyone else:
    received → investing, paid → financing. And a pure non-cash transaction (shares
    issued to a vendor for machinery, debentures converted to equity) never enters the
    statement at all — it is disclosed separately.
  </p>
</Callout>

**Direct vs indirect method.** The direct method reports gross receipts from customers
and gross payments to suppliers/employees; the indirect method starts from net profit and
un-does non-cash items and working-capital movements. Both end at the same cash from
operations; only the operating section differs.

## 7. Building the statement

The indirect-method drill, in order:

1. Start with **net profit before tax and extraordinary items**.
2. Add back non-cash charges (depreciation, amortisation, provisions) and items belonging
   to other activities (interest expense → add back here, show under financing; profit on
   sale of assets/investments → deduct, the proceeds go to investing).
3. Adjust **working capital**: increase in current assets consumes cash (deduct);
   increase in current liabilities releases cash (add).
4. Deduct **taxes on income actually paid** → cash from operating activities.
5. Build investing and financing sections from gross movements (a machinery account often
   has to be reconstructed to find purchases: closing + depreciation + WDV of disposals −
   opening).
6. Total of the three sections = net increase/decrease in cash and cash equivalents;
   prove it against opening and closing balances.

### Worked example 3 — indirect method, all three sections

Periyar Chemicals: net profit before tax ₹6,50,000; depreciation ₹1,20,000; profit on
sale of investments ₹20,000; interest expense ₹45,000. Debtors rose ₹70,000, inventory
fell ₹40,000, creditors rose ₹25,000. Tax paid ₹1,80,000. It bought PPE for ₹3,00,000,
sold investments (book value ₹1,00,000) for ₹1,20,000, raised a loan of ₹2,00,000, paid
dividend ₹90,000 and paid the interest of ₹45,000.

Operating before working capital: 6,50,000 + 1,20,000 − 20,000 + 45,000 = ₹7,95,000.
After working capital: 7,95,000 − 70,000 + 40,000 + 25,000 = ₹7,90,000. Less tax paid →
**CFO ₹6,10,000**. Investing: −3,00,000 + 1,20,000 = **−₹1,80,000** (the ₹20,000 profit
is inside the ₹1,20,000 proceeds — never count it twice). Financing: +2,00,000 − 90,000 −
45,000 = **+₹65,000**. Net increase in cash: 6,10,000 − 1,80,000 + 65,000 = **₹4,95,000**.

## 8. The chapter in one paragraph

Books at the registered office, eight years, accrual and double entry (s.128); financial
statements are the s.2(40) five-piece set (OPC/small/dormant may drop the cash flow);
true and fair in Schedule III's shape (s.129). Division I: vertical balance sheet on the
current/non-current axis, operating cycle as the yardstick (twelve months by default),
share capital reconciliation, >5% holders, negative surplus stays in reserves & surplus,
contingent liabilities in notes, mandatory rounding. P&L by nature. AS 3: cash
equivalents = three months from acquisition; three activities; interest/dividends flip
between financial and other enterprises; overdrafts are financing; non-cash deals stay
out; indirect method un-does accruals from net profit and lands on the same CFO as the
direct method.
