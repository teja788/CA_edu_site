Two companies earn the same cash from the same trade — and report different profits,
because one capitalises what the other expenses. Accounting standards exist to kill that
divergence. This opening chapter is pure orientation: what a standard is, who writes it
and by what procedure, how many are in force, and how India's two parallel rulebooks —
the ASs this paper uses and the IFRS-converged Ind ASs — divide the corporate world
between them. It is the most fact-dense, least numerical chapter in the paper: exam
questions here are won by remembering *which body, which year, which threshold*.

## 1. Why accounting standards exist

**Accounting standards** are written policy documents, issued by an expert body or the
government, covering four aspects of an accounting transaction or event: its
**recognition** (when it enters the accounts), **measurement** (at what amount),
**presentation** (where it sits in the statements) and **disclosure** (what must be said
about it in the notes).

Their purpose is to standardise diverse accounting policies with twin aims: making
financial statements **comparable** — between entities and between periods — and giving
users information that is credible, transparent and prepared on a known basis.

The benefits worth listing: divergent practices are eliminated within the permitted
range; management gets less room for creative accounting; statements become comparable;
and users (investors, lenders, regulators) can rely on a common language. The
**limitations** are the mirror image: where a standard permits alternative treatments,
the very divergence it fights survives inside it; standards bring a degree of rigidity to
unusual situations; and a standard **cannot override the statute** — where an Act
prescribes a treatment, the Act prevails and the standard yields.

## 2. Who sets the standards, and how

The ICAI constituted the **Accounting Standards Board (ASB)** in **1977** to formulate
standards. Its composition is deliberately broad — council members, industry, regulators
(MCA, SEBI, RBI, CAG, CBDT), academics — so a draft is argued over by preparers, auditors
and users before it hardens into a rule.

The procedure runs in a fixed sequence worth memorising:

1. ASB **identifies the area** and constitutes study groups to prepare a preliminary
   draft.
2. The draft circulates among Council members and specified outside bodies for comment.
3. An **exposure draft** is issued for **public comment**.
4. The ASB finalises the standard in light of comments and submits it to the **Council
   of the ICAI**, which modifies (in consultation with ASB) and issues it.

For **companies**, that ICAI standard is not law until notified: the **Central
Government** prescribes accounting standards under **s.133 of the Companies Act 2013**,
on the recommendation of the ICAI, in consultation with the **National Financial
Reporting Authority (NFRA)**. The current notification is the **Companies (Accounting
Standards) Rules, 2021** (23 June 2021), which superseded the 2006 Rules. For
**non-corporate entities** (firms, LLPs, trusts), the ASs issued by the ICAI itself apply,
enforced through its members who audit them.

## 3. The AS rulebook today

The ICAI has issued **29** Accounting Standards in all. Two are gone: **AS 6**
(Depreciation Accounting) was **withdrawn when AS 10 was revised** into Property, Plant
and Equipment, and **AS 8** (Accounting for Research and Development) was **withdrawn on
the issuance of AS 26** (Intangible Assets). So **27 standards are effectively in
force**. Several — AS 2, 4, 10, 13, 14, 21 and 29 — have been revised to pull them closer
to their Ind AS counterparts.

## 4. The IFRS family

Globally listed capital wants one accounting language, and the **International
Accounting Standards Board (IASB)** supplies it. What the world loosely calls "IFRS" is
really a four-part family: **IFRS** issued by the IASB itself; **IAS** issued by its
predecessor (the IASC) and carried forward; and the interpretations — **IFRIC** (of the
IFRS Interpretations Committee) and **SIC** (of the earlier Standing Interpretations
Committee).

A country can become IFRS-compliant two ways. **Adoption** means applying IFRS as issued
by the IASB, word for word. **Convergence** means writing national standards that are
aligned with IFRS but allow departures where local law, economics or practice demand
them. India chose **convergence**, not adoption.

## 5. Ind AS: convergence, carve-outs and the roadmap

The **Indian Accounting Standards (Ind AS)** are India's IFRS-converged rulebook,
notified under the **Companies (Indian Accounting Standards) Rules, 2015** — again under
s.133. Their numbering deliberately mirrors the international family (Ind AS 109 answers
IFRS 9; Ind AS 2 answers IAS 2), so a reader can line the two up instantly.

Because convergence is not adoption, Ind AS depart from IFRS in places. A **carve-out**
removes or changes an IFRS requirement that would not suit Indian conditions; a
**carve-in** adds an option or guidance that IFRS does not contain. These departures are
the price of fitting a global template onto local law.

**The roadmap** (companies other than banks, NBFCs and insurers):

- **Voluntary** from FY beginning 1 April 2015, with comparatives — and adoption is a
  one-way door: **once Ind AS applies, there is no going back**.
- **Phase I, mandatory from 1 April 2016**: companies listed (or in the process of
  listing) in India or abroad with **net worth ₹500 crore or more**; unlisted companies
  with net worth ₹500 crore or more; and the holding, subsidiary, joint venture and
  associate companies of all of these.
- **Phase II, mandatory from 1 April 2017**: **all remaining listed companies** (except
  those listed only on SME exchanges); unlisted companies with net worth of **₹250 crore
  or more but under ₹500 crore**; plus their holding/subsidiary/JV/associate companies.
- **SME-exchange-listed companies are outside the roadmap** and stay on AS unless they
  opt in.
- NBFCs joined later (net worth ≥ ₹500 crore from 1 April 2018; listed and other
  specified NBFCs from 1 April 2019); banks and insurers follow their regulators'
  timelines.

### Walkthrough — which rulebook for which company?

An unlisted trading company, net worth ₹320 crore → Phase II threshold (≥ ₹250 crore)
caught it from 1 April 2017 → **Ind AS**, and its subsidiaries came along. An unlisted
company with net worth ₹180 crore and no Ind AS group ties → below every threshold →
**AS under the 2021 Rules**. A company listed only on an SME exchange → **AS**, however
large its market enthusiasm, unless it volunteers. And the volunteer who adopted Ind AS
in 2015 can never return to AS.

## 6. Which rulebook this paper uses

CA Intermediate Advanced Accounting prepares financial statements under the **ASs of the
Companies (Accounting Standards) Rules, 2021** (and ICAI ASs for non-corporates). The
Ind AS material in this chapter is orientation — the full Ind AS treatment belongs to the
Final course. When a question here says "the company complies with Accounting Standards",
it means the 27-standard AS rulebook, not Ind AS.

## 7. The chapter in one paragraph

Standards fix recognition, measurement, presentation and disclosure so statements compare
across entities and years; they cannot override statute, and permitted alternatives are
their built-in leak. ASB (1977) drafts through study groups and an exposure draft; the
ICAI Council issues; the Central Government notifies for companies under s.133 in
consultation with NFRA — currently the AS Rules 2021. Twenty-nine issued, AS 6 and AS 8
withdrawn (into revised AS 10 and AS 26), twenty-seven standing. IFRS = IFRS + IAS +
IFRIC + SIC, written by the IASB; adoption copies, convergence aligns — India converged
into Ind AS (2015 Rules), with carve-outs/ins, numbering that mirrors IFRS, and a roadmap:
voluntary 2015, ₹500-crore/listed Phase I in 2016, remaining-listed/₹250-crore Phase II in
2017, SME-exchange companies exempt, and no exit once in.
