Four standards share this chapter, and each answers a question about **something that
lands in — or reshapes — the financial statements after the books think they are
done**. AS 4 asks what to do with events that happen *after* the balance sheet date but
*before* the statements are approved: adjust, or merely tell the reader? AS 5 polices
how the profit and loss statement is **classified and disclosed** — ordinary versus
extraordinary, this year's estimates versus last year's errors, a changed estimate
versus a changed policy — so that profit figures stay comparable across years and
enterprises. AS 11 converts a multi-currency world into rupees: which rate, which
item, which destination for the difference. And AS 22 forces income tax onto the
accrual and matching principles — the tax expense of a year is the tax **effect** of
that year's transactions, current and deferred, not merely the cheque written to the
department.

Applicability shorthand (the regime lives in
[Ch 3](/intermediate/advanced-accounting/applicability-of-accounting-standards/)):
**AS 4 and AS 5 bind every entity in full** — no SMC or MSME relaxation exists for
either. **AS 11** also applies in its entirety to everyone; the only concession is that
MSMEs may skip the *encouraged* disclosures of para 44 (foreign currency risk
management policy), which were voluntary anyway. **AS 22** applies in full to all
companies, SMCs included; a non-company **MSME complies for current tax only**, and on
first availing that relief adjusts the accumulated deferred tax balance against
opening revenue reserves.

## 1. AS 4: Contingencies and Events Occurring After the Balance Sheet Date

### What is left of "contingencies"

The title promises two subjects, but the **contingencies half of AS 4 stands largely
superseded**: with AS 29 mandatory, the contingency paragraphs remain operational only
to the extent they cover **impairment of assets not dealt with by other standards**.
Provisions and contingent liabilities live in AS 29 ([Ch 6](/intermediate/advanced-accounting/liabilities-based-as/));
what this unit really teaches is the second half — events after the balance sheet
date.

### The window and the two species

**Events occurring after the balance sheet date** are those significant events, both
**favourable and unfavourable**, that occur between the **balance sheet date** and the
**date on which the financial statements are approved** — by the **Board of
Directors** for a company, and by the corresponding approving authority for any other
entity. An event outside this window is not the subject of AS 4 at all.

Within the window, everything splits into two species:

- **Adjusting events** provide **additional evidence of, or further clarify,
  conditions that existed at the balance sheet date**. Assets and liabilities are
  **adjusted**. Classic cases: a debtor whose balance stood at the year end goes
  **insolvent** shortly after (the financial trouble existed at the year end — the
  insolvency merely confirms it); a post-year-end **sale below cost** that evidences
  the year-end net realisable value of inventory; the **settlement of a court case**
  that confirms an obligation which existed at the balance sheet date.
- **Non-adjusting events** reflect **conditions that arose after** the balance sheet
  date — a fire or flood after the year end, a fall in the market value of investments
  after the year end, a strike, a business combination struck in the new year. **No
  adjustment** is made, because the statements portray the position as it stood *on*
  the balance sheet date.

Non-adjusting events are not simply buried, though. Where they represent **material
changes and commitments affecting the financial position** of the enterprise,
disclosure is made in the **report of the approving authority** — the Directors'
Report, for a company. The disclosure gives the **nature of the event** and an
**estimate of its financial effect, or a statement that such an estimate cannot be
made**.

<Callout kind="example">
Vaigai Traders' year ends 31 March; the Board approves the accounts on 28 June. A
customer owing ₹6,00,000 at the year end is declared insolvent on 25 April, and the
liquidator expects a recovery of 25 paise per rupee. The insolvency confirms a
condition — the customer's inability to pay — that existed at the balance sheet date,
so this is an **adjusting event**: expected recovery = 6,00,000 × 0.25 = ₹1,50,000, and
a **provision of ₹4,50,000** (6,00,000 − 1,50,000) is made in the 31 March accounts. A
fire that destroys a godown on 10 May, by contrast, is a condition born after the year
end: **no adjustment**, but being material it is disclosed in the report of the
approving authority.
</Callout>

### The two exceptions the exam loves

**Dividends.** The rule since the 2016 amendment: if dividends are **declared after
the balance sheet date**, the enterprise does **not recognise them as a liability** at
the balance sheet date — a proposed dividend is not a present obligation, because
declaration (shareholder approval at the AGM) has not happened. Proposed dividends are
**disclosed in the notes** to the financial statements, and the **liability is
recorded in the year the dividend is declared**. (Older books — and older exam answers
— provided for proposed dividends as an adjusting statutory item; that position is
dead.)

**Going concern.** Events after the balance sheet date normally never touch the
accounts unless the condition existed at the year end — with one carve-out. Where a
post-year-end event indicates that the **enterprise ceases to be a going concern** —
the destruction of a major production plant by fire after the balance sheet date, or a
severe deterioration in operating results and financial position — the **fundamental
accounting assumption of going concern is displaced**, and the statements themselves
are redrawn on an appropriate basis. Here an after-date condition does change the
numbers, not merely the notes.

<Callout kind="mistake">
Two traps sit side by side. First: proposed dividend is **not** a liability — it goes
to the **notes**, and becomes a liability only on declaration at the AGM. Second:
non-adjusting events are disclosed in the **report of the approving authority**
(Directors' Report), not in the notes to the financial statements — the notes route
for a non-adjusting event is the wrong answer, except for the dividend disclosure
which the standard expressly sends to the notes.
</Callout>

## 2. AS 5: Net Profit or Loss for the Period, Prior Period Items and Changes in Accounting Policies

### Why classification is the whole game

AS 5 exists so that the statement of profit and loss is **prepared and presented on a
uniform basis** — profit comparable with the enterprise's own past and with other
enterprises. It requires classification and disclosure of extraordinary and prior
period items, disclosure of certain items within ordinary profit, and a discipline for
changes in estimates and policies. **All items of income and expense recognised in a
period enter the determination of net profit or loss for the period** unless an
accounting standard requires or permits otherwise.

Net profit or loss has **two components, each disclosed on the face** of the
statement: **profit or loss from ordinary activities**, and **extraordinary items**.

### Ordinary, exceptional in size, extraordinary

**Ordinary activities** are those undertaken as part of the business and the related
activities incidental to it. When an item of ordinary income or expense is of such
**size, nature or incidence** that its disclosure is relevant to explaining
performance, its **nature and amount are disclosed separately** — but it stays
ordinary. The standard's own list: **write-down of inventories to NRV** and its
reversal, **restructuring provisions** and their reversals, **disposals of items of
fixed assets**, **disposals of long-term investments**, **legislative changes having
retrospective application**, **litigation settlements**, and other reversals of
provisions.

**Extraordinary items** are income or expenses arising from events or transactions
**clearly distinct from the ordinary activities** of the enterprise and therefore
**not expected to recur frequently or regularly** — losses from an **earthquake**, or
the **seizure of assets by government**. They are disclosed in the statement of profit
and loss **as part of net profit or loss**, with the nature and amount of each shown
separately so their effect on current profit can be perceived. Context matters:
crop damage from hailstorms may be extraordinary for most enterprises, yet ordinary
for an agricultural enterprise in a hail-prone belt.

### Prior period items

**Prior period items** are income or expenses that arise **in the current period as a
result of errors or omissions in the preparation of the financial statements of one or
more prior periods** — mathematical mistakes, misapplication of accounting policies,
misinterpretation of facts, items simply overlooked. Two boundaries: an error must be
distinguished from a **change in estimate** (estimates are approximations that get
revised as better information arrives — revisions are *not* errors), and prior period
items are normally **infrequent**.

The treatment is the one Ind AS students find strange: **no restatement of prior-year
comparatives**. The item is included in the determination of the **current period's**
net profit or loss, with **separate disclosure** such that its impact on the current
profit or loss can be perceived — either as a separate line after profit from ordinary
activities, or within the relevant ordinary heads with the prior-period amount
disclosed by note.

### Changes in accounting estimates

Estimation is inherent — bad debts, inventory obsolescence, useful lives and residual
values of depreciable assets. When circumstances change or new information arrives, a
revision is a **change in accounting estimate**, and its effect is recognised
**prospectively**: in the **period of the change**, or in the **period of the change
and future periods** where the change affects both (a revised useful life changes
depreciation for the current *and* each remaining period). The effect keeps the **same
classification** the estimate had before, and the **nature and amount** of a material
change is disclosed — or, if quantification is impracticable, that fact.

<Callout kind="example">
Krishna Mills bought a machine for ₹15,00,000, depreciated straight-line over ten
years (no residual value): ₹1,50,000 a year. At the start of year 7, wear and market
evidence cut the remaining useful life to **two years**. Carrying amount after six
years = 15,00,000 − (6 × 1,50,000) = **₹6,00,000**; depreciation for each of years 7
and 8 = 6,00,000 ÷ 2 = **₹3,00,000**. Years 1–6 are never touched — a change in
estimate works only forwards.
</Callout>

### Changes in accounting policies

A change in an accounting policy is allowed **only** if:

1. it is **required by statute**;
2. it is required for **compliance with an accounting standard**; or
3. it would result in a **more appropriate presentation** of the financial statements.

Any change with a **material effect** is disclosed; the **impact of, and adjustments
resulting from, the change** are shown in the financial statements of the period of
change **to the extent ascertainable** — where not ascertainable, that fact is stated.
If a change has no material effect now but is reasonably expected to have one in later
periods, the fact of the change is disclosed in the period of adoption. Where a new
standard carries **transitional provisions**, the changeover follows them.

Two look-alikes are **not** policy changes: adopting a policy for events or
transactions that **differ in substance** from earlier ones, and adopting a policy for
events or transactions that **did not occur previously or were immaterial**. And where
a change cannot be cleanly labelled policy or estimate, it is **treated as a change in
estimate**, with appropriate disclosure.

<Callout kind="mistake">
A **change in the method of depreciation** is a change in accounting **estimate**, not
policy — AS 10 (2016) settled this — so it applies **prospectively** with AS 5
estimate disclosure. And a prior period item is **never** fixed by restating last
year's published figures: under AS 5 the correction runs through the **current**
period's statement with separate disclosure. Both are standing exam traps.
</Callout>

## 3. AS 11: The Effects of Changes in Foreign Exchange Rates

### Transactions in foreign currency

AS 11 (Revised 2003) covers **accounting for foreign currency transactions**,
**translating the financial statements of foreign operations**, and **forward exchange
contracts**. (Exchange differences treated as an adjustment to interest cost belong to
AS 16.) A foreign currency transaction is **recorded initially at the exchange rate on
the date of the transaction**; an **average rate** for a week or month may be used if
rates do not fluctuate significantly.

At **each balance sheet date** the rules split by the nature of the item:

- **Monetary items** — money held, and assets and liabilities to be received or paid
  in **fixed or determinable amounts of money** (cash, receivables, payables, loans) —
  are reported at the **closing rate**. (If the closing rate is unrealistic because of
  remittance restrictions, use the rate likely to be realised.)
- **Non-monetary items carried at historical cost** — fixed assets, inventories,
  investments in equity shares — stay at the **rate on the date of the transaction**.
- **Non-monetary items carried at fair value** use the rate on the **date the fair
  value was determined**.

**Exchange differences** on monetary items — on settlement, or on restating them at
rates different from those previously recorded — are recognised as **income or expense
in the period they arise**, in the statement of profit and loss.

<Callout kind="example">
Tapti Traders imports raw material worth **$75,000** on 15 January when the rate is
₹82.80: purchases and creditor recorded at 75,000 × 82.80 = **₹62,10,000**. The
creditor is unpaid at 31 March, closing rate ₹84.00. The creditor (monetary) is
restated to 75,000 × 84.00 = ₹63,00,000, and the **exchange loss of ₹90,000**
(75,000 × 1.20) goes to the **P&L**. The inventory (non-monetary, at cost) **stays at
₹62,10,000** — the closing rate never touches it.
</Callout>

**The para 46A option.** For **long-term foreign currency monetary items** (twelve
months or more at origination), a company may elect — irrevocably, with disclosure —
to take exchange differences somewhere other than the P&L: differences relating to the
acquisition of a **depreciable capital asset** are **added to or deducted from the
asset's cost** and depreciated over its remaining life; other long-term differences
accumulate in a **Foreign Currency Monetary Item Translation Difference Account
(FCMITDA)** and are **amortised over the balance period** of the monetary item.
Without the election, everything goes to the P&L as usual.

### Foreign operations: integral vs non-integral

A foreign operation is classified by how it functions relative to the reporting
enterprise:

- An **integral foreign operation** carries on business **as if it were an extension**
  of the reporting enterprise's own operations — a sales branch that sells goods
  imported from head office and remits the proceeds. Its activities move the reporting
  enterprise's own cash flows more or less directly.
- A **non-integral foreign operation** accumulates cash and other monetary items,
  incurs expenses, generates income and arranges borrowings **substantially in its
  local currency**; transactions with the reporting enterprise are a **low
  proportion** of its activities; its sales prices, costs and markets are chiefly
  local. A change in exchange rate affects the reporting enterprise's **net
  investment**, not its individual cash flows.

The classification drives the translation method — and, crucially, **where the
exchange difference goes**:

| | Integral | Non-integral |
|---|---|---|
| Method | Translate **as if the transactions were the reporting enterprise's own** | Translate the operation **as a whole** |
| Monetary items | Closing rate | Closing rate |
| Non-monetary items | **Transaction-date rate** (cost) or fair-value-date rate | **Closing rate** — every asset and liability |
| Income and expenses | Transaction-date rates (average allowed) | Transaction-date rates (average allowed) |
| Exchange differences | **Statement of profit and loss** | **Foreign currency translation reserve (FCTR)**, held until disposal |

A monetary item receivable from or payable to a non-integral operation, for which
**settlement is neither planned nor likely in the foreseeable future**, is in
substance part of the **net investment** — exchange differences on it go to the
**FCTR** in the consolidated financial statements. On the **disposal** of a
non-integral operation, the **cumulative FCTR balance is recognised as income or
expense in the same period as the gain or loss on disposal**. When classification
changes: integral → non-integral, the non-integral procedures apply from the date of
change and differences on translating non-monetary assets at that date go to FCTR;
non-integral → integral, the translated amounts at the date of change become the
**historical costs**, and the accumulated FCTR waits until the operation is disposed
of.

### Forward exchange contracts

The accounting splits by purpose:

- **Hedging (not for trading or speculation).** The **premium or discount** — the
  difference between the **contracted forward rate and the spot rate at inception** —
  is **amortised as expense or income over the life of the contract**. **Exchange
  differences on the contract** (the movement in the spot rate applied to the foreign
  currency amount) are recognised in the **P&L of the period in which rates change**.
  Profit or loss on **cancellation or renewal** goes to the P&L of that period.
- **Trading or speculation.** No premium or discount is amortised at all. The contract
  is **marked to market**: the gain or loss is the difference between the **forward
  rate available at the reporting date for the remaining maturity** and the
  **contracted forward rate**, applied to the foreign currency amount — recognised in
  the P&L for the period.

<Callout kind="example">
On 1 February, Penna Exports (hedging a payable) buys **$60,000** six months forward at
₹84.10 when the spot rate is ₹83.50. Premium = (84.10 − 83.50) × 60,000 = **₹36,000**,
amortised over the six-month life — the year ending 31 March absorbs two months:
36,000 × 2/6 = **₹12,000 expense**. Spot at 31 March is ₹83.90, so the exchange
difference on the contract = (83.90 − 83.50) × 60,000 = **₹24,000 gain**, recognised in
the P&L now. Had the same contract been speculative, the ₹36,000 premium would never
be amortised — the P&L would simply take the difference between the contracted rate
and the 31 March forward rate for the remaining maturity.
</Callout>

<Callout kind="mistake">
Restating **everything** at the closing rate is the classic AS 11 error — closing rate
belongs to **monetary items** (and to *all* items only of a **non-integral**
operation). Inventory, PPE and equity investments carried at cost keep their
transaction-date rate. The mirror trap: sending a non-integral operation's translation
difference to the P&L — it accumulates in **FCTR** and touches the P&L only on
disposal.
</Callout>

## 4. AS 22: Accounting for Taxes on Income

### Why deferred tax exists

Taxable income and accounting income diverge because tax law and accounting standards
recognise items differently. AS 22 applies **accrual and matching to income tax
itself**: the **tax expense** of a period = **current tax + deferred tax**, both
included in determining the period's net profit or loss. Current tax is the amount of
income tax determined to be payable on the **taxable income** of the period; deferred
tax is the **tax effect of timing differences**.

The divergences come in two species:

- **Permanent differences** originate in one period and **never reverse** — a penalty
  disallowed by tax law forever, agricultural income exempt forever, the disallowed
  slice of a donation. They change the effective tax rate of the year and are
  otherwise ignored: **no deferred tax, ever**.
- **Timing differences** originate in one period and are **capable of reversal** in
  one or more subsequent periods — depreciation differing in rate or method between
  books and tax, **section 43B** expenses (bonus, statutory dues) deducted for tax
  only on payment, provision for doubtful debts allowed only on actual write-off,
  preliminary expenses amortised over five years under section 35D, and **unabsorbed
  depreciation and carried-forward losses** — the tax law itself postpones their
  deduction to future years.

Timing differences that will produce **taxable amounts later** (tax depreciation
running ahead of book depreciation) create a **deferred tax liability (DTL)**; those
that will produce **deductible amounts later** (expenses disallowed now, allowed
later) create a **deferred tax asset (DTA)**.

### Prudence: the two-step certainty ladder

DTLs are recognised for **all** timing differences. DTAs climb a ladder:

1. **General rule** — recognise a DTA **only to the extent there is reasonable
   certainty** that sufficient future taxable income will be available against which
   it can be realised.
2. **Unabsorbed depreciation or carry-forward of losses under tax laws** — recognise a
   DTA **only to the extent there is virtual certainty supported by convincing
   evidence** that sufficient future taxable income will be available. Convincing
   evidence means something concrete and demonstrable at the balance sheet date — a
   binding sales contract or order book expected to generate the profits — not an
   optimistic projection.

Unrecognised DTAs are **re-assessed at each balance sheet date** and recognised when
the certainty test comes to be met; recognised DTAs are **written down** when the
certainty evaporates, and the write-down is **reversed** if it returns.

<Callout kind="example">
Sharavati Ltd reports accounting profit of ₹20,00,000 for the year. It includes a
pollution-law **penalty of ₹1,00,000** (disallowed — permanent), a **provision for
doubtful debts of ₹60,000** (allowed for tax only on write-off — timing), and book
depreciation of ₹3,00,000 against tax depreciation of ₹4,40,000 (**timing difference
₹1,40,000**). Tax rate 30%. Taxable income = 20,00,000 + 1,00,000 + 60,000 − 1,40,000
= **₹20,20,000**; **current tax = ₹6,06,000**. Deferred tax: DTL on depreciation =
1,40,000 × 30% = ₹42,000; DTA on doubtful debts = 60,000 × 30% = ₹18,000; **net
deferred tax expense ₹24,000**. Total tax expense = 6,06,000 + 24,000 = **₹6,30,000**
— exactly 30% of (20,00,000 + 1,00,000), because the only difference that never
reverses is the permanent one.
</Callout>

### Measurement, tax holidays, presentation

**Measurement.** Current tax is measured at the amount expected to be paid using the
**applicable tax rates and tax laws**; deferred tax uses the **rates and laws enacted
or substantively enacted by the balance sheet date**. Where different rates apply to
different levels of taxable income, use **average rates**. Deferred tax assets and
liabilities are **never discounted** to present value. Where **MAT** (section 115JB)
applies, the MAT amount **is** the current tax of the period — deferred tax continues
to be measured at the regular enacted rates.

**Tax holidays** (sections 10A/10B, 80-IA territory). Deferred tax on timing
differences that **originate and reverse within the holiday period** is **not
recognised** — their reversal is tax-free anyway. Timing differences that will
**reverse after the holiday ends** are recognised **in the year they originate**. For
ordering, the differences that originated **first are considered to reverse first**
(FIFO).

**Presentation and offsetting.** Deferred tax assets and liabilities are
**distinguished from current assets and liabilities** and from advance tax, and shown
under a separate heading in the balance sheet. An enterprise **offsets current tax
assets against current tax liabilities** only when it has a legally enforceable right
of set-off and intends to settle net; it **offsets DTAs against DTLs** only when a
legally enforceable right of set-off for current amounts exists **and** both relate to
taxes levied by the **same governing taxation laws**. Disclosures: the **break-up of
deferred tax into major components**, and — where the enterprise has unabsorbed
depreciation or carried-forward losses — the **nature of the evidence** supporting the
DTA recognised.

<Callout kind="mistake">
The certainty ladder is the most-tested line in AS 22, and candidates flatten it.
An ordinary DTA (doubtful debts, 43B items) needs **reasonable** certainty; a DTA born
of **unabsorbed depreciation or carried-forward losses** needs **virtual certainty
supported by convincing evidence** — a profit forecast alone is not convincing
evidence. Two further slips: deferred tax is computed **only on timing differences**
(never on permanent ones), and DTA/DTL balances are **never discounted**, however
distant the reversal.
</Callout>

## 5. The chapter in one paragraph

AS 4 watches the window between the balance sheet date and the approval of the
statements: events confirming conditions that existed at the year end **adjust** the
accounts (an insolvent debtor, NRV evidence, a settled suit); events born after the
year end do not — they are disclosed in the **report of the approving authority** if
material — with two carve-outs: dividends declared after the year end are **not a
liability** (notes disclosure, recognised on declaration), and an event destroying the
**going concern** assumption redraws the statements themselves. AS 5 sorts the P&L:
ordinary activities (abnormal-size items separately disclosed but still ordinary),
**extraordinary items** (clearly distinct, not expected to recur — earthquake,
expropriation) shown separately within net profit, **prior period items** (errors and
omissions of earlier years — through the current P&L with separate disclosure, no
restatement), estimate changes applied **prospectively**, and policy changes only when
statute, a standard or more appropriate presentation demands — with the impact
quantified and disclosed. AS 11 records transactions at the transaction-date rate,
restates **monetary items at closing rate** (differences to P&L, subject to the
para 46A long-term option), leaves cost-carried non-monetary items alone, translates
**integral operations as if they were the enterprise's own** (differences to P&L) and
**non-integral operations wholly at closing rate** (differences parked in **FCTR**
until disposal), amortises forward-contract **premium over the contract life for
hedges**, and **marks speculative contracts to market** against the forward rate for
the remaining maturity. AS 22 splits tax expense into current and deferred, computes
deferred tax **only on timing differences**, recognises every DTL but admits DTAs on a
prudence ladder — **reasonable certainty** generally, **virtual certainty with
convincing evidence** for unabsorbed depreciation and carried-forward losses — measures
at **enacted or substantively enacted** rates without discounting, skips deferred tax
on differences reversing inside a **tax holiday**, reviews DTAs each year, and offsets
only within the **same governing taxation laws**.
