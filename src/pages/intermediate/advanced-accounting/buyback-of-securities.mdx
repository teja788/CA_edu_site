A company usually takes money IN from shareholders. A buyback is the reverse gear: the company
pays money OUT to purchase its own shares and cancel them. Fewer shares remain, each remaining
share owns a bigger slice, and the balance sheet shrinks. Because paying capital back to owners
is exactly what creditor-protection law exists to police, the Companies Act 2013 wraps buyback
in tight conditions — and the exam tests whether you can apply those conditions *and* pass the
journal entries, with working notes an examiner can follow.

This chapter is a dependable scorer: one full numerical (maximum permissible buyback, or
entries + balance-sheet extract) plus conceptual MCQs on the limits and timelines. Every
numerical follows the same three-test pattern shown in Worked Example 1. Learn the pattern
and the working-note format, and there is nothing left to surprise you.

## 1. What a buyback is, and why companies do it

**Buyback (purchase of own securities)** is a company acquiring its own shares or other
specified securities from existing holders, against payment, followed by cancellation of what
it bought. The power comes from **section 68(1)** of the Companies Act 2013 — a company may
purchase its own shares or other specified securities out of:

- its **free reserves**,
- its **securities premium account**, or
- the **proceeds of an earlier issue** of shares or other specified securities — **but never**
  money raised by earlier issuing the *same kind* of shares (s.68(1) proviso). You cannot
  issue fresh equity today to buy back equity tomorrow.

Why do it? Returning surplus cash, supporting a share price seen as undervalued, improving
return-on-equity and EPS by shrinking the equity base, or adjusting the capital structure.
For the exam you need the *mechanics*, not the strategy — but a stem will often dress the
motive in a story, so read past it to the numbers.

<Callout kind="pointer">
  <p>
    <strong>"Free reserves" has TWO layers here — the exam's favourite trap.</strong> Generally,
    s.2(43) excludes the securities premium account (and revaluation reserve, and unrealised
    gains) from free reserves. But the Explanation to s.68 says that <em>for buyback purposes</em>
    "free reserves" <strong>includes the securities premium account</strong>. So when you compute
    the 25%/10% limits and the debt-equity test, securities premium joins the base; revaluation
    reserve never does. Outside s.68 — say, declaring dividend — s.2(43) rules and premium is out.
  </p>
</Callout>

## 2. The rulebook: conditions under s.68(2)

No buyback is valid unless ALL of these hold:

| Condition | Rule | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Authorisation | Articles must authorise the buyback | s.68(2)(a) |
| Approval | **Special resolution** in general meeting; a **board resolution** alone suffices if the buyback is **10% or less** of paid-up equity capital + free reserves | s.68(2)(b) |
| Size ceiling | Buyback ≤ **25% of paid-up share capital + free reserves** (free reserves here **includes securities premium** — s.68 Explanation) in a financial year; for equity buybacks the 25% is also measured against **total paid-up equity capital** as a shares-count test | s.68(2)(c) |
| Leverage | Post-buyback **debt ≤ 2 × (paid-up capital + free reserves)** — the 2:1 debt-equity ceiling. "Debt" = secured **and** unsecured | s.68(2)(d) |
| Quality | Only **fully paid-up** shares can be bought back | s.68(2)(e) |
| Procedure | Listed companies follow SEBI regulations; unlisted companies follow Rule 17 of the Share Capital and Debentures Rules 2014 | s.68(2)(f)–(g) |

Two follow-on timing rules sit in the same section:

- **One offer a year:** no fresh buyback offer within **one year** of the closure of the
  preceding offer (proviso to s.68(2)).
- **Complete in a year:** every buyback must be **completed within one year** of the special
  (or board) resolution — s.68(4).

<Callout kind="mistake">
  <p>
    <strong>The 10% and the 25% answer different questions.</strong> 10% of (paid-up equity
    capital + free reserves) is the ceiling for the <em>board route</em> — skip the general
    meeting. 25% of (paid-up capital + free reserves) is the absolute yearly ceiling
    <em>with</em> a special resolution. A stem that says "the board alone approved" caps the
    company at 10%, however much headroom the 25% test shows.
  </p>
</Callout>

## 3. After the cheque: extinguishment and the quiet period

- **Extinguish within 7 days:** shares bought back must be extinguished and physically
  destroyed within **seven days** of the last date of completion — s.68(7).
- **No fresh issue for 6 months:** after a buyback, the company cannot make a *further issue
  of the same kind of shares* within **six months**, except bonus shares or conversions
  (ESOPs, sweat equity, convertible instruments already outstanding) — s.68(8).
- **Register of buyback** (SH-10) and, before the buyback, a **declaration of solvency**
  (SH-9) signed by two directors — s.68(5)–(6), (9); Rule 17 for unlisted companies.

## 4. Prohibitions — s.70

A company must NOT buy back its shares:

1. **through any subsidiary**, including its own subsidiaries;
2. **through investment companies**, singly or as a group; or
3. while a **default subsists** in repaying deposits or interest on them, redeeming debentures
   or preference shares, paying dividend, or repaying a term loan (or interest) to a bank or
   financial institution. If the default has been **remedied**, the ban continues for
   **three years** after the default ceased — s.70(1) proviso.

Buyback is also barred while the company has not complied with **s.92** (annual return),
**s.123** (declaration of dividend), **s.127** (failure to distribute dividend) or **s.128**
(books of account) — s.70(2).

## 5. Accounting: the entries and the CRR

The accounting has exactly four moves. Suppose shares of face value *F* are bought back at
price *P* (so premium on buyback = *P − F*):

1. **Make the payment due:**
   `Equity share capital A/c Dr (F)` · `Premium on buyback A/c Dr (P − F)` →
   `To Equity shareholders A/c (P)`
2. **Fund the premium** — write it off against **securities premium** first (to the extent
   available), balance against **free reserves**:
   `Securities premium A/c Dr` · `General reserve / P&L A/c Dr` → `To Premium on buyback A/c`
3. **Pay:** `Equity shareholders A/c Dr` → `To Bank A/c`
4. **Capital redemption reserve (s.69):** where the buyback is out of free reserves or
   securities premium, transfer **a sum equal to the nominal value of the shares bought
   back** to CRR: `General reserve / P&L A/c Dr` → `To Capital redemption reserve A/c`.
   If part of the buyback is funded by the **proceeds of a fresh issue** (of a different
   kind), CRR = nominal value bought back **minus** those proceeds.

The CRR exists to stop capital escaping through the back door: distributable reserves equal to
the cancelled capital are locked away. CRR can be applied **only** for issuing **fully paid
bonus shares** — s.69(2).

<Callout kind="mistake">
  <p>
    <strong>CRR is the nominal value, never the amount paid.</strong> Buy back 40,000 shares
    of ₹10 at ₹15 out of free reserves and the CRR transfer is ₹4,00,000 — not ₹6,00,000.
    The premium is a separate write-off (entry 2). Mixing the two is the single most common
    error in this chapter.
  </p>
</Callout>

### Worked Example 1 — journal entries (the standard pattern)

Meridian Foods Ltd buys back 30,000 equity shares of ₹10 each at ₹25 per share. The balance
sheet shows securities premium of ₹3,00,000 and general reserve of ₹9,00,000. No fresh issue
is made.

**Working Note 1 — amounts.** Nominal value = 30,000 × 10 = **₹3,00,000**. Payment = 30,000 ×
25 = **₹7,50,000**. Premium on buyback = 30,000 × 15 = **₹4,50,000**.

**Working Note 2 — funding the premium.** Securities premium available ₹3,00,000 is used
first; the balance ₹1,50,000 comes from general reserve.

**Working Note 3 — CRR.** No fresh issue, so CRR = full nominal value = **₹3,00,000**, from
general reserve.

| Journal | Dr (₹) | Cr (₹) |
|---|---|---|
| Equity share capital A/c Dr | 3,00,000 | |
| Premium on buyback A/c Dr | 4,50,000 | |
| — To Equity shareholders A/c | | 7,50,000 |
| Securities premium A/c Dr | 3,00,000 | |
| General reserve A/c Dr | 1,50,000 | |
| — To Premium on buyback A/c | | 4,50,000 |
| Equity shareholders A/c Dr | 7,50,000 | |
| — To Bank A/c | | 7,50,000 |
| General reserve A/c Dr | 3,00,000 | |
| — To Capital redemption reserve A/c | | 3,00,000 |

Check the drain on general reserve: 1,50,000 (premium) + 3,00,000 (CRR) = ₹4,50,000, leaving
₹4,50,000 — comfortably inside the ₹9,00,000 available. Always show this check; it is where
examiners look first.

### Worked Example 2 — buyback partly out of a fresh issue

Orbit Textiles Ltd buys back 50,000 equity shares of ₹10 at ₹18, partly funded by a fresh
issue of 20,000 preference shares of ₹10 at par (a *different kind* of security — permitted
as a source).

**Working Note 1.** Nominal value bought back = 50,000 × 10 = ₹5,00,000. Payment = 50,000 ×
18 = ₹9,00,000. Premium = ₹4,00,000, written off against securities premium / free reserves
as available.

**Working Note 2 — CRR with a fresh issue.** CRR = nominal value bought back − proceeds of
the fresh issue = 5,00,000 − 2,00,000 = **₹3,00,000**. The fresh-issue portion of the capital
cancelled is already replaced by new capital, so only the free-reserve-funded portion needs
locking into CRR.

## 6. The maximum-buyback pattern (the exam's favourite numerical)

Whenever a stem asks "what is the maximum number of shares the company can buy back?", run
**three tests and take the smallest answer**. Show each test as its own working note.

- **Test 1 — shares outstanding:** 25% of the number of paid-up equity shares.
- **Test 2 — resources:** 25% of (paid-up capital + free reserves), divided by the buyback
  price per share.
- **Test 3 — debt-equity:** post-buyback, debt ≤ 2 × shareholders' funds. Maximum cash that
  can leave = shareholders' funds − (total debt ÷ 2). Divide by price for the share count.

### Worked Example 3 — maximum permissible buyback

Zenith Gears Ltd (unlisted, Pune) has: paid-up equity capital ₹20,00,000 (2,00,000 shares of
₹10); general reserve ₹18,00,000; surplus in P&L ₹4,00,000; secured term loan ₹40,00,000;
unsecured loan ₹14,00,000. The board proposes a buyback at ₹28 per share with a special
resolution in place. Maximum shares?

**Working Note 1 — shares-outstanding test.** 25% × 2,00,000 = **50,000 shares**.

**Working Note 2 — resources test.** Free reserves = 18,00,000 + 4,00,000 = ₹22,00,000
(no securities premium exists here; had there been one, it would join the base — s.68
Explanation). Base = 20,00,000 + 22,00,000 = ₹42,00,000. 25% = ₹10,50,000. At ₹28:
10,50,000 ÷ 28 = **37,500 shares**.

**Working Note 3 — debt-equity test.** Debt = 40,00,000 + 14,00,000 = ₹54,00,000. Minimum
post-buyback equity = 54,00,000 ÷ 2 = ₹27,00,000. Maximum outflow = 42,00,000 − 27,00,000 =
₹15,00,000. At ₹28: **53,571 shares** (rounded down).

**Conclusion.** Maximum buyback = **min(50,000; 37,500; 53,571) = 37,500 shares**, costing
37,500 × 28 = ₹10,50,000. Had only the board approved (no special resolution), the ceiling
would drop to 10%: ₹4,20,000 ÷ 28 = 15,000 shares.

  <p>
    At Intermediate the working notes ARE the answer. Label each test, show the base, the
    percentage, the division by price, and the explicit min() conclusion. An unlabelled
    correct figure earns less than a labelled trail an examiner can tick line by line.
  </p>
</Callout>

## 7. Presentation and the wider map

After the buyback, the balance sheet (Schedule III) shows the reduced **equity share
capital**, the new **capital redemption reserve** under Reserves & Surplus, and the drained
securities premium / free reserves. Disclose the buyback in the notes to accounts.

Keep buyback distinct from its neighbours:

- **Reduction of capital (s.66):** a court-supervised (NCLT) route that can touch *any*
  capital, including unpaid amounts; buyback is a self-administered purchase of fully paid
  shares within s.68 limits — no tribunal.
- **Redemption of preference shares (s.55):** the scheduled retirement of a class issued as
  redeemable; buyback is discretionary and targets equity or other specified securities.
- **ESOP trust purchases / treasury shares:** Indian law requires extinguishment within seven
  days — bought-back shares can never be held as treasury stock.

That's the chapter. The three-test pattern (WE 3), the four journal moves (WE 1), and the
CRR-with-fresh-issue twist (WE 2) cover every numerical the SM asks; the limits table and
s.70 list cover the MCQs.
