Before any individual standard says how to account for inventory or leases, something
has to say what an *asset* even is, when anything gets recognised at all, and what makes
one set of financial statements better than another. That something is the **Framework**
— the constitution under the statutes. It never overrides a standard (when the two
conflict, **the Accounting Standard prevails**), but every standard is drafted inside its
vocabulary, and exam questions mine it for definitions with a precision that punishes
paraphrase.

## 1. What the Framework is for

The Framework lays down the concepts on which external-purpose financial statements are
built. It exists to assist the standard-setters in
developing and reviewing standards, to help preparers apply standards and handle topics
no standard yet covers, to help auditors form an opinion on whether statements conform,
and to help users interpret what they read.

Two boundary facts matter: the Framework **is not itself an Accounting Standard**, and
in any conflict between the two, **the Standard wins**. The components of a full set of
financial statements in its world: balance sheet, statement of profit and loss, cash
flow statement, and the notes and schedules that form part of them. Their objective:
to tell a wide range of users where the enterprise stands (**financial position**), how
it fared (**performance**) and where its **cash flows** went — usefully enough to support
economic decisions by investors, employees, lenders, suppliers, customers, government and
the public.

## 2. The three fundamental assumptions

The Framework names three **fundamental accounting assumptions**: **going concern**,
**accrual** and **consistency**.

- **Going concern** — the enterprise will continue operating in the foreseeable future;
  there is neither intention nor need to materially curtail operations. This is what
  justifies carrying assets at cost rather than fire-sale values, retaining profit to
  replace consumed assets, and providing for liabilities in orderly course. Prepare on
  any other basis (say, net realisable values for a dying business) and that basis must
  be disclosed.
- **Accrual** — effects of transactions are recognised **when they occur**, not when
  cash moves, and are recorded in the periods to which they relate.
- **Consistency** — accounting policies are followed uniformly from one period to the
  next. A change is permitted where a statute or standard requires it, or where the new
  policy presents the statements more appropriately.

<Callout kind="pointer">
  <p>
    <strong>The disclosure rule is the exam's favourite inversion.</strong> If the three
    assumptions ARE followed, <em>no disclosure is required</em> — silence means
    compliance. Only a DEPARTURE from a fundamental assumption must be specifically
    disclosed. Candidates regularly reverse this.
  </p>
</Callout>

## 3. What makes information useful

Four principal **qualitative characteristics** make financial information useful:

1. **Understandability** — comprehensible to users with reasonable knowledge of
   business, who study the information diligently.
2. **Relevance** — capable of influencing decisions; bound up with **materiality** (an
   item is material if its misstatement or omission could influence decisions —
   a threshold judged by size *and* nature, not a fixed percentage).
3. **Reliability** — dependable information, which unpacks into: **faithful
   representation** of what actually happened; **substance over form** (account for the
   economic reality, not the legal packaging — the hire-purchase buyer records the
   asset before title passes); **neutrality** (free from bias); **prudence** (do not
   anticipate profits, provide for all known losses — but prudence never licenses
   deliberate understatement or hidden reserves, which would breach neutrality); and
   **completeness**.
4. **Comparability** — across periods and entities, which is why policies are disclosed
   and applied consistently.

Financial statements are commonly said to show a **true and fair view**; the Framework's
position is that applying the principal qualitative characteristics and the applicable
standards ordinarily achieves exactly that.

## 4. The five elements, and when they enter the books

The Framework defines five elements. Learn them verbatim — options in MCQs differ by
single words:

- **Asset**: a resource **controlled** by the enterprise **as a result of past events**,
  from which **future economic benefits are expected to flow** to the enterprise.
  Control, not ownership, is the operative test.
- **Liability**: a **present obligation** arising from **past events**, the settlement
  of which is expected to result in an **outflow of resources** embodying economic
  benefits.
- **Equity**: the **residual interest** in the assets after deducting all liabilities —
  never independently defined, always what is left.
- **Income**: increases in economic benefits in the form of inflows/enhancements of
  assets or decreases of liabilities that increase equity, other than contributions from
  owners. It embraces both **revenue** (ordinary activities) and **gains**.
- **Expense**: decreases in economic benefits that decrease equity, other than
  distributions to owners — embracing ordinary expenses and **losses**.

**Recognition** — actually posting an element to the balance sheet or P&L — needs two
tests met together: it is **probable** that future economic benefits associated with the
item will flow to or from the enterprise, and the item has a **cost or value that can be
measured reliably**. An item that fails either test may still warrant note disclosure,
but it stays off the face.

## 5. Measurement bases

The Framework recognises **four** measurement bases, usable in varying combinations:

| Basis | Assets carried at | Liabilities carried at |
|---|---|---|
| **Historical cost** | Amount paid (or fair value of consideration given) at acquisition | Proceeds received in exchange for the obligation, or amounts expected to be paid to settle |
| **Current cost** | What it would cost to acquire the same or equivalent asset **now** | Undiscounted amount needed to settle **now** |
| **Realisable (settlement) value** | Amount obtainable in an orderly disposal now | Undiscounted amount expected to be paid in the normal course |
| **Present value** | Discounted value of future net cash inflows the item is expected to generate | Discounted value of future net cash outflows expected to settle |

Indian standards run **largely on historical cost**, with pointed exceptions — the most
quoted being inventory, acquired at historical cost but carried at year end at the
**lower of cost and net realisable value**.

## 6. Capital maintenance: has the business kept its capital?

Capital means the **net assets** of the business. Profit is only genuine if the closing
net assets exceed what the business needed to maintain its opening capital — dividends
paid out of anything less are a quiet liquidation. The SM's algebra:

**P = (CA − CL) − (OA − OL) − C + D**, where P is profit, CA/CL closing assets and
liabilities, OA/OL opening ones, C capital introduced and D drawings or dividends.
Retained profit (P − D) must not be negative — closing equity must at least equal
opening equity plus capital introduced. The bite comes from *how you value* the capital
to be maintained:

1. **Financial capital maintenance at historical cost** — opening and closing equity at
   historical cost. Adequate only while prices stand still.
2. **Financial capital maintenance at current purchasing power** — opening equity
   restated by a **general price index** to closing prices; profit is what exceeds the
   restated capital. Better, but average indices hide asset-specific price moves.
3. **Physical capital maintenance** — capital is the **operating capability**: profit
   exists only after providing what it costs **to replace** the assets consumed, at
   current/replacement cost.

### Worked example 1 — the retained-profit formula

Closing assets ₹9,00,000, closing liabilities ₹3,40,000; opening assets ₹7,00,000,
opening liabilities ₹2,80,000; capital introduced during the year ₹50,000; drawings
₹90,000. P = (9,00,000 − 3,40,000) − (7,00,000 − 2,80,000) − 50,000 + 90,000 =
**₹1,80,000**. Retained profit = 1,80,000 − 90,000 = ₹90,000 ≥ 0, so capital (at
historical cost) is maintained.

### Worked example 2 — one trader, three verdicts

A trader starts the year with ₹20,000, being 5,000 units bought at ₹4. He sells them
all at ₹5 (₹25,000) and withdraws ₹3,000. At the year end the unit's replacement price
is ₹4.60 and the general price index has moved 100 → 110.

- *Historical cost*: profit = 25,000 − 20,000 = **₹5,000**; after drawings, retained
  ₹2,000 — capital maintained.
- *Current purchasing power*: capital to maintain = 20,000 × 110/100 = ₹22,000; profit =
  25,000 − 22,000 = **₹3,000**; after drawings ₹3,000, retained is exactly nil — just
  maintained.
- *Physical*: replacing 5,000 units now costs 5,000 × 4.60 = ₹23,000; profit = 25,000 −
  23,000 = **₹2,000**; drawings of ₹3,000 leave retained profit at −₹1,000 — the
  operating capability has NOT been maintained, and ₹1,000 of the drawing was capital
  dressed as income.

Same till, same cash — three different verdicts, which is the whole point: the choice of
capital-maintenance concept decides what counts as profit at all.

## 7. The chapter in one paragraph

The Framework guides standard-setters, preparers, auditors and users, and yields to any
conflicting AS. Three fundamental assumptions — going concern, accrual, consistency —
are presumed unless departure is disclosed. Useful information is understandable,
relevant (materiality), reliable (faithful representation, substance over form,
neutrality, prudence without hidden reserves, completeness) and comparable; together
with the standards this delivers the true and fair view. Five elements — asset
(controlled resource, past events, future benefits), liability (present obligation,
expected outflow), equity (residual), income, expense — are recognised when the benefit
flow is probable and the amount reliably measurable. Four measurement bases (historical,
current, realisable, present value), with historical cost dominant in AS practice. And
capital maintenance asks whether profit is real: at historical cost, at general
purchasing power, or as physical operating capability — the same year can pass one test
and fail another.
