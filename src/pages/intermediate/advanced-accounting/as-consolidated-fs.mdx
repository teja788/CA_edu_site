One economic entity can hide behind many legal ones. A parent that parks debt, stock
or losses in a subsidiary shows its shareholders a separate balance sheet that is
technically true and economically misleading — which is why the law makes the group
report **as if it were a single enterprise**. Three standards divide the territory by
**how much say the investor has**. **Control** → AS 21: consolidate the subsidiary
**line by line**, showing outsiders' slice as minority interest. **Significant
influence** (typically 20%+) → AS 23: carry the associate by the **equity method** —
one line that grows with the investor's share of profits. **Joint control** → AS 27:
bring in the venturer's **proportionate share of each line** of a jointly controlled
entity. Same three questions each time: what relationship exists, what goes into the
consolidated statements, and what happens to profits the group has only made against
itself.

Applicability shorthand (the regime lives in
[Ch 3](/intermediate/advanced-accounting/applicability-of-accounting-standards/)):
none of the three standards itself compels anyone to prepare CFS — each **applies
when an enterprise presents consolidated financial statements**. The compulsion is
statutory: **section 129(3)** of the Companies Act, 2013 requires a company with one
or more **subsidiaries** — the Explanation extends the word to **associate companies
and joint ventures** — to prepare CFS, subject to the Rule 6 carve-outs (most
famously the unlisted intermediate wholly-owned subsidiary whose parent files CFS in
India). Non-company entities under the revised ICAI scheme face no CFS mandate, but
the moment any enterprise chooses (or is told) to present CFS, AS 21/23/27 govern it
in full — these standards carry **no SMC/MSME measurement relaxations**.

## 1. AS 21: Consolidated Financial Statements

### Control, parent, subsidiary

**Control** under AS 21 has two alternative limbs:

1. ownership, **directly or indirectly through subsidiary(ies)**, of **more than
   one-half of the voting power** of an enterprise; **or**
2. **control of the composition of the board of directors** (or corresponding
   governing body) **so as to obtain economic benefits** from its activities.

Either limb alone is enough. A **parent** is an enterprise with one or more
subsidiaries; a **subsidiary** is an enterprise controlled by a parent; the **group**
is a parent with all its subsidiaries. Board composition is controlled when the
enterprise can **appoint or remove all or a majority of directors without the consent
or concurrence of any other person** — including where a person's appointment as
director follows necessarily from his appointment in the parent, or where the
director is a nominee of the enterprise or its subsidiary.

Two consequences the exam probes. First, **control can run through a chain**: if H
holds 60% of A and A holds 55% of B, then B is a subsidiary of H — through A — even
though H's *economic* interest in B is only 33%. Voting control, not effective
shareholding, decides who consolidates. Second, in **rare cases both limbs can point
to two different enterprises** — one holding the majority voting power, another
controlling the board. AS 21 accepts the oddity: **both are parents, and both
consolidate** the same subsidiary.

Consolidation sweeps in **all subsidiaries, domestic and foreign**, with exactly
**two exclusions**:

- **control is intended to be temporary** — the subsidiary was acquired and is held
  **exclusively with a view to its subsequent disposal in the near future**
  (ordinarily **not more than twelve months** from acquisition, unless a longer
  period can be justified on the facts); or
- the subsidiary operates under **severe long-term restrictions which significantly
  impair its ability to transfer funds to the parent**.

An excluded subsidiary is carried in the CFS as an **investment under AS 13**, with
the reason for non-consolidation disclosed. Nothing else excuses exclusion — a
subsidiary whose business is **dissimilar** to the group's is still consolidated;
better information comes from consolidating and explaining through **AS 17 segment
disclosures**, not from leaving it out.

CFS are presented **in addition to, not instead of,** the parent's separate financial
statements — the statute wants both sets, and in the parent's **separate** books the
subsidiary remains an AS 13 investment.

### The mechanics: line by line, then three cuts

Consolidation starts by **combining like items line by line** — assets, liabilities,
income, expenses of parent and subsidiaries added together — and then makes three
surgical adjustments so the group isn't counting itself:

1. **Eliminate the parent's investment against its portion of the subsidiary's equity
   at the date the investment was made.** The difference is **goodwill** (cost above
   the share of equity — an asset in the CFS) or **capital reserve** (share of equity
   above cost). Where the investment came in tranches, the comparison is made
   **step by step** at each date. If statements as at the acquisition date are
   impracticable, the immediately preceding financial statements are used, adjusted
   for significant intervening transactions.
2. **Minority interest in the year's profit or loss** is identified and adjusted
   against the group's income, so the consolidated P&L attributes to the parent's
   owners only what belongs to them.
3. **Minority interest in net assets** is computed and shown in the consolidated
   balance sheet **separately from liabilities and from the equity of the parent's
   shareholders**. It consists of the minority's share of equity **at the date of
   investment** plus its share of **movements in equity since** that date.

<Callout kind="example">
H Ltd acquires 80% of S Ltd on 1 April 20X1 for ₹6,00,000, when S's share capital is
₹5,00,000 and its reserves and surplus stand at ₹1,50,000. S's equity at acquisition
= 5,00,000 + 1,50,000 = ₹6,50,000; H's 80% share = ₹5,20,000. **Goodwill = 6,00,000 −
5,20,000 = ₹80,000**, shown as an asset in the CFS. Had H paid only ₹4,80,000 for the
same stake, the ₹40,000 by which its share of equity exceeds cost would be a
**capital reserve** instead.
</Callout>

### Pre-acquisition vs post-acquisition profits

The acquisition date slices the subsidiary's reserves in two. Profits earned **up to
the date of investment** are **capital profits** for the group: the parent effectively
bought them, so they enter the cost-of-control computation (and the minority's share
of them sits inside minority interest) — they never reach consolidated revenue
reserves. Profits earned **after** acquisition are **revenue profits**: the parent's
share joins consolidated reserves and the consolidated P&L; the minority's share
joins minority interest. Where the parent acquires mid-year and the subsidiary's
profit accrues evenly, the year's profit is **time-apportioned** around the
acquisition date. A **dividend received out of pre-acquisition profits** follows the
same logic — it is a **recovery of cost**, credited against the investment, never
income of the parent.

<Callout kind="example">
Continuing the H–S example: at 31 March 20X3, S's share capital is ₹5,00,000 and its
reserves ₹2,70,000, of which ₹1,50,000 existed at acquisition. **Minority interest =
20% × (5,00,000 + 2,70,000) = ₹1,54,000** — the minority takes its share of the
subsidiary's *entire* equity at the balance sheet date, pre- and post-acquisition
alike. Of the post-acquisition growth of ₹1,20,000, the parent's 80% share of
**₹96,000** flows into consolidated reserves; the pre-acquisition ₹1,50,000 stays
locked in the goodwill computation done at acquisition.
</Callout>

<Callout kind="mistake">
Two date errors sink more consolidation answers than anything else. Goodwill is
measured against the subsidiary's equity **at the date of investment** — recomputing
it with balance-sheet-date reserves manufactures a fictitious capital reserve as the
subsidiary earns profits. Minority interest is the opposite: it is the minority's
share of equity **at the balance sheet date** — freezing it at acquisition-date
equity strips the minority of its share of post-acquisition growth.
</Callout>

### Intragroup elimination — in full

Intragroup balances (mutual owings, loans) and intragroup transactions (sales,
expenses, dividends) are **eliminated in full**, together with **unrealised profits**
sitting in assets the group still holds — closing stock bought from a fellow group
member, fixed assets transferred at a markup. Under AS 21 the elimination is **in
full whether the sale ran downstream (parent to subsidiary) or upstream (subsidiary
to parent)** — the group as a single entity has simply moved goods from one pocket to
another. **Unrealised losses** are also eliminated in full, **unless the cost cannot
be recovered** — a genuine fall in value stays recognised.

<Callout kind="example">
S Ltd (an 80% subsidiary) sold goods to H Ltd at cost plus 25%. H's closing stock
includes ₹1,00,000 of these goods. Unrealised profit = 1,00,000 × 25/125 =
**₹20,000**, eliminated **in full** against consolidated stock (carried at
₹80,000 — its cost to the group) even though the sale was upstream and outsiders own
20% of the seller. The margin fraction matters: 25% *on cost* is 25/125 of the
invoice value, not 25% of it.
</Callout>

### Housekeeping rules the exam loves

- **Uniform accounting policies.** Consolidation uses uniform policies for like
  transactions; if that is **not practicable**, the CFS disclose **that fact together
  with the proportions of the items** to which different policies were applied.
- **Reporting dates.** The statements consolidated should be drawn up to the same
  date; where they are not, adjust for **significant events** between the dates, and
  the gap may **never exceed six months**.
- **Taxes.** The parent's and each subsidiary's tax expenses are simply
  **aggregated** — no notional recomputation for the group.
- **Losses and the minority.** Losses applicable to the minority may **exceed the
  minority's interest** in the subsidiary's equity. The excess (and further losses)
  is **adjusted against the majority interest**, unless the minority has a **binding
  obligation to make good the losses** and can do so. If the subsidiary later turns
  profitable, **all profits are allocated to the majority until the minority's share
  of previously absorbed losses is recovered**. So: minority interest of ₹80,000
  facing a minority loss share of ₹1,10,000 is reported at **nil**, ₹30,000 falls on
  the majority, and of a later minority profit share of ₹50,000 the first ₹30,000
  restores the majority — minority interest re-emerges at ₹20,000.
- **Cessation.** Consolidation of a subsidiary's results runs **until the date the
  parent-subsidiary relationship ceases**; the gain or loss on disposal is the
  difference between the proceeds and the carrying amount of the net assets disposed
  of (with related goodwill). From that date the retained investment follows
  **AS 13**, and its **carrying amount on that date is regarded as cost thereafter**.
- **Disclosure.** A list of all subsidiaries with country of
  incorporation/residence, proportion of ownership and (if different) of voting
  power; the nature of the relationship where the parent holds one-half or less of
  the voting power; the effect of acquisitions and disposals during the period; and
  the reporting dates used where they differ from the parent's.

## 2. AS 23: Accounting for Investments in Associates in Consolidated Financial Statements

### Significant influence and the associate

An **associate** is an enterprise in which the investor has **significant
influence** and which is **neither a subsidiary nor a joint venture** of the
investor. Significant influence is the **power to participate in the financial
and/or operating policy decisions** of the investee **without control** over those
policies. The working presumption: holding **20% or more of the voting power**,
directly or **indirectly through subsidiary(ies)**, indicates significant influence
**unless clearly demonstrated otherwise**; below 20%, the presumption runs the other
way — again **rebuttable**. Another investor's majority stake does not by itself
destroy significant influence. Evidence that can rebut either way: **representation
on the board**, **participation in policy-making**, **material transactions** between
the parties, **interchange of managerial personnel**, or **provision of essential
technical information**.

Scope is the standard's sharpest edge: AS 23 operates **only when the investor
prepares and presents consolidated financial statements**. A **non-company** investor
with no subsidiary prepares no CFS, so it never applies the equity method — its
associates sit at **cost under AS 13** in its own statements. A **company** cannot
use that escape: s.129(3) read with its Explanation compels CFS even where the only
group relationship is an associate, and the associate is equity-accounted in those
CFS. Even an investor that does prepare
CFS carries associates at AS 13 cost **in its separate financial statements**; the
equity method lives exclusively in the consolidated set.

### The equity method

Under the **equity method** the investment is **initially recorded at cost**,
identifying any **goodwill or capital reserve** arising at acquisition (cost versus
the investor's share of the associate's net assets). Thereafter the carrying amount
moves with the investor's world:

> carrying amount = cost
> **plus** the investor's share of post-acquisition profits (or minus losses)
> **plus/minus** the investor's share of post-acquisition reserve movements
> (revaluations, foreign-exchange translation)
> **minus** distributions (dividends) received from the associate.

The consolidated P&L shows the **investor's share of the associate's results** —
one line, presented separately, with the share of any extraordinary or prior period
items also separately disclosed. The goodwill or capital reserve identified at
acquisition is **included in the carrying amount of the investment** — never shown as
a separate balance-sheet asset — but **disclosed separately**. The investment itself
appears as a **long-term investment** in the consolidated balance sheet.

<Callout kind="example">
A Ltd (which prepares CFS) acquires 30% of B Ltd on 1 April 20X1 for ₹4,00,000 when
B's net assets are ₹12,00,000. Share of net assets = 30% × 12,00,000 = ₹3,60,000, so
the cost includes **goodwill of ₹40,000** (disclosed, not separated). In 20X1–X2 B
earns ₹2,00,000 and pays dividends of ₹50,000. Carrying amount at 31 March 20X2 =
4,00,000 + 30% × 2,00,000 − 30% × 50,000 = 4,00,000 + 60,000 − 15,000 =
**₹4,45,000**. The consolidated P&L carries ₹60,000 as share of the associate's
profit; the ₹15,000 dividend is not income — it shrinks the investment.
</Callout>

Two exemptions mirror AS 21: an associate acquired and held **exclusively for
disposal in the near future**, or operating under **severe long-term restrictions
impairing fund transfers to the investor**, is not equity-accounted — it stays at
**AS 13 cost even in the CFS**, with the reason disclosed. The equity method is also
**discontinued** from the date the investor **ceases to have significant influence**
(or such restrictions arise): the carrying amount on that date becomes **cost**
thereafter.

### Losses, eliminations, mechanics

When the investor's **share of losses equals or exceeds the carrying amount**, the
investment is reported at **nil** and the investor **discontinues recognising further
losses** — additional losses are provided only to the extent the investor has
**incurred obligations or made payments on the associate's behalf** (guarantees and
the like). If the associate returns to profit, the investor resumes recognition
**only after its share of profits equals the share of net losses not recognised**.

**Unrealised profits** on transactions with an associate — upstream or downstream —
are eliminated **only to the extent of the investor's interest** in the associate
(the associate is an outsider for the rest); unrealised losses likewise, unless the
cost of the transferred asset cannot be recovered. The most recent available
financial statements of the associate are used, drawn where practicable to the
investor's reporting date, with adjustments for significant events in any gap; for
uniform policies, adjust — and where that is impracticable, **disclose the fact**.

<Callout kind="mistake">
Two directions to keep straight against AS 21. First, the elimination fraction:
subsidiaries lose intragroup profit **in full**; associates only **to the extent of
the investor's interest** — carrying the 100% habit into AS 23 overstates the
elimination. Second, the venue: the equity method **never appears in separate
financial statements** — an investor that equity-accounts its associate in its own
standalone books has confused AS 23 with AS 13.
</Callout>

## 3. AS 27: Financial Reporting of Interests in Joint Ventures

### Joint control and the three forms

A **joint venture** is a contractual arrangement whereby two or more parties
undertake an economic activity **subject to joint control** — the **contractually
agreed sharing of control**, such that no single venturer can control the activity
unilaterally. The **contractual arrangement** (a contract, minutes, articles —
usually in writing) is what separates a joint venture from an associate: it
typically covers the activity and duration, appointments to the governing body,
capital contributions, and the sharing of output, income, expenses or results. A
**venturer** is a party with joint control; an **investor in a joint venture** is a
party without it.

AS 27 sorts every joint venture into **three forms**, each with its own accounting —
and for the first two the treatment is identical in the venturer's **separate and
consolidated** statements, because the items involved are already the venturer's own:

1. **Jointly controlled operations (JCO)** — each venturer uses its **own** assets
   and resources (an aircraft built by consortium members, each making a part). The
   venturer recognises **the assets it controls and the liabilities and expenses it
   incurs**, plus **its share of the income** from the joint activity.
2. **Jointly controlled assets (JCA)** — joint ownership of one asset (a pipeline
   shared by oil companies). The venturer recognises its **share of the joint
   assets, classified by the nature of the asset** — not as an investment — any
   **liabilities it has incurred** and its **share of joint liabilities**, its
   **share of the output or income** together with its **share of joint expenses**,
   and expenses it incurred itself.
3. **Jointly controlled entities (JCE)** — a separate company, partnership or other
   entity in which each venturer holds an interest.

### The JCE split: AS 13 alone, proportionate consolidation in CFS

For a JCE the venue decides the method. In the venturer's **separate financial
statements**, the interest is an **investment accounted for under AS 13**. In the
venturer's **consolidated financial statements**, the interest is reported by
**proportionate consolidation**: the venturer's **share of each of the assets,
liabilities, income and expenses** of the JCE is included, **line by line** — either
combined with the venturer's own like items or shown as separate line items — on a
**gross basis, with no offsetting** of assets against liabilities or income against
expenses. A **non-company** venturer with no subsidiary prepares no CFS, so it never
proportionately consolidates — but a company venturer is pulled into CFS by
s.129(3)'s Explanation (joint ventures count) and proportionately consolidates
there. Proportionate consolidation starts when joint control begins and
**ceases from the date joint control ends** — thereafter AS 21, AS 23 or AS 13
applies according to what relationship survives. The AS 21-style exemptions repeat:
an interest held **exclusively for near-future disposal**, or a JCE under **severe
long-term restrictions**, stays at AS 13 cost.

<Callout kind="example">
V Ltd (which prepares CFS) holds a 40% interest in W Ltd, a jointly controlled
entity. W's statements show fixed assets ₹10,00,000, inventories ₹4,00,000,
creditors ₹3,00,000 and revenue ₹20,00,000. V's CFS include **₹4,00,000 of fixed
assets, ₹1,60,000 of inventories, ₹1,20,000 of creditors and ₹8,00,000 of
revenue** — 40% of every line, merged with (or shown beside) V's own figures. No
one-line "investment in W" and no single "share of W's profit" caption: the whole
skeleton of W enters at 40%.
</Callout>

<Callout kind="mistake">
Under AS 27 a jointly controlled entity in CFS takes **proportionate consolidation,
not the equity method** — equity-accounting a JCE is the classic cross-standard
error (it is Ind AS 111 that abolished proportionate consolidation; the AS regime
keeps it). The mirror-image error also costs marks: applying proportionate
consolidation in the venturer's **separate** statements, where AS 13 alone governs.
</Callout>

### Transactions with the venture, investors, operators

When a venturer **contributes or sells assets to** a joint venture and the assets
are retained by the venture, substance rules: while the significant risks and
rewards have passed, the venturer recognises **only that portion of the gain
attributable to the interests of the other venturers**. Its own share of the profit
is unrealised — it has partly sold to itself. A **loss**, though, is recognised **in
full** when it evidences a reduction in net realisable value or an impairment. When
a venturer **purchases assets from** a joint venture, it does not recognise **its
share of the venture's profit** on the sale **until it resells the asset to an
independent party** — losses again immediately if they reflect NRV declines or
impairment.

An **investor** in a joint venture — a participant without joint control — stands
outside these rules: it reports its interest under **AS 13** (or, if it in fact has
control or significant influence, under AS 21 or AS 23). **Operators or managers**
of a venture treat their fees as **revenue under AS 9**; to the venture the fee is
an expense.

Disclosures: the venturer's **contingent liabilities and capital commitments**
relating to its joint-venture interests, shown separately; a **list of joint
ventures** with a description of interests and the proportion of ownership in each
JCE; and, for proportionately consolidated JCEs, the **aggregate amounts of each of
current assets, long-term assets, current liabilities, long-term liabilities, income
and expenses** related to those interests.

## 4. The chapter in one paragraph

AS 21 consolidates every controlled enterprise — control being majority voting power
(directly or through subsidiaries) or command of the board, with only
temporary-control and severe-restriction subsidiaries left out as AS 13
investments — by adding line items in full, cancelling the parent's investment
against acquisition-date equity to throw up goodwill or capital reserve, splitting
the subsidiary's reserves into capital (pre-acquisition) and revenue
(post-acquisition) profits, carrying the minority's balance-sheet-date share of
equity separately from liabilities and parent equity, eliminating intragroup
balances and unrealised profits in full both ways, demanding uniform policies (or
disclosure), capping reporting-date gaps at six months, and pushing minority losses
beyond the minority's equity onto the majority unless the minority is bound to make
them good. AS 23 takes the 20%-presumption associate and, only inside CFS, runs the
equity method — cost plus post-acquisition share of profits and reserves minus
dividends, goodwill or capital reserve buried in the carrying amount but disclosed,
losses recognised only down to nil (then only against obligations), unrealised
profits eliminated only to the investor's interest — while separate statements keep
AS 13 cost. AS 27 splits joint ventures three ways: jointly controlled operations
and assets put each venturer's own items and shares straight into its books
everywhere, while a jointly controlled entity is an AS 13 investment in separate
statements and proportionately consolidated — line-by-line share, never the equity
method — in CFS; gains on sales to the venture are recognised only for the other
venturers' portion, purchases hold profit until resale outside, investors without
joint control fall back to AS 13/21/23, and operators' fees are AS 9 revenue.
