Seven standards share this chapter, and one idea unites them: none of them changes *how
much* profit you make — they change **what the reader gets told**. AS 1 makes you confess
your accounting policies; AS 3 re-sorts the year into three streams of cash; AS 17 slices
the enterprise into segments; AS 18 names the parties who could bend a deal; AS 20
standardises the most-quoted number in finance; AS 24 forces early warning of a business
being shut down; AS 25 disciplines the quarterly report. Presentation and disclosure —
not measurement — is the battleground, and the exam mines each standard's definitions
and thresholds.

Applicability shorthand for the whole chapter (the detailed regime lives in
[Ch 3](/intermediate/advanced-accounting/applicability-of-accounting-standards/)):
**AS 1 binds everyone.** Non-company **MSMEs skip AS 3, AS 17, AS 20 and AS 24
entirely**, and the smaller MSME sub-class skips AS 18 too. **SMCs skip AS 17 and the
diluted-EPS disclosure but still prepare a cash flow statement** — a company's cash-flow
exemption comes from s.2(40) of the Companies Act (OPC, small company, dormant company),
not from the AS regime. AS 25 binds only an enterprise that is *required or elects* to
publish an interim report.

## 1. AS 1: Disclosure of Accounting Policies

Standardisation never eliminates choice: standards cannot cover every area, and no
single policy suits every enterprise. **Accounting policies** are the specific accounting
principles and the methods of applying them that an enterprise adopts in preparing its
statements. Familiar areas where policies legitimately differ: depreciation methods,
inventory valuation, treatment of goodwill, valuation of investments, retirement
benefits, recognition of profit on long-term contracts, valuation of fixed assets.

Three **fundamental accounting assumptions** — going concern, consistency, accrual — are
presumed. The disclosure rule is one-directional: **say nothing if they are followed;
disclose the fact if any is not**. (The Framework chapter covers what each assumption
means; AS 1 only legislates the disclosure.)

Selection of a policy is governed by one master test — the statements should present a
**true and fair** view — served by three considerations:

- **Prudence** — do not anticipate profits; provide for all known liabilities and
  losses even if the amount needs estimation.
- **Substance over form** — account for the financial reality of a transaction, not
  merely its legal shape.
- **Materiality** — disclose every item whose knowledge might influence a user's
  decisions.

The disclosure discipline itself:

- All **significant accounting policies should be disclosed in one place**, as part of
  the financial statements — not scattered through the notes.
- A **change** in policy that has a material effect must be disclosed with the **amount
  of the effect** to the extent ascertainable; if not ascertainable, **that fact** must
  be stated.
- A change with no material effect now but one **reasonably expected in later periods**
  must still be disclosed in the period of the change.

<Callout kind="mistake">
Disclosure is not detergent. A **wrong or inappropriate treatment is not remedied by
disclosing the policy** or by a note — AS 1 makes this explicit. You cannot depreciate
nothing, footnote it candidly, and call the statements true and fair.
</Callout>

## 2. AS 3: Cash Flow Statements

Profit is an opinion; cash is a fact. AS 3 explains where the year's cash came from and
went, classified into **operating**, **investing** and **financing** activities. The
statement reconciles opening to closing **cash and cash equivalents** — cash in hand,
demand deposits, and investments that are **short-term (maturity of, say, three months
or less from acquisition), highly liquid, readily convertible into known amounts of
cash, and subject to insignificant risk of change in value**.

| Stream | What it holds | Signature items |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Operating | Principal revenue-producing activities | Cash from customers, payments to suppliers and employees, taxes on income |
| Investing | Acquisition/disposal of long-term assets and investments outside cash equivalents | Purchase/sale of PPE and investments, loans made and recovered |
| Financing | Changes in the size/composition of owners' capital and borrowings | Share/debenture proceeds, repayments, dividends paid |

Operating flows can be reported by the **direct method** (gross receipts less gross
payments) or the **indirect method** (net profit adjusted for non-cash items, deferrals
and accruals, and items belonging to investing/financing). Both give the same subtotal;
only the presentation of operating flows differs.

The classification traps the exam loves:

- **Interest and dividends** — for a **financial enterprise**, interest paid and
  interest/dividends received are **operating**. For every other enterprise, interest
  and dividends **received are investing**; interest **paid** and dividends **paid are
  financing**. Dividends paid are financing for everyone.
- **Taxes on income** are **operating** unless specifically identifiable with an
  investing or financing transaction (securities transaction tax on a sale of
  investments rides with the investing flow; dividend distribution levies ride with the
  financing flow).
- **Extraordinary items** are shown **separately within the activity** to which they
  belong (an earthquake insurance recovery for stock loss: operating, disclosed
  separately).
- **Non-cash transactions** — shares issued for machinery, debentures converted to
  equity — are **excluded** from the statement and disclosed elsewhere.
- **Foreign currency flows** are recorded at the **rate on the date of the flow**;
  exchange gains/losses on cash balances are shown as a separate reconciling item, not
  as an operating/investing/financing flow.
- **Net-basis reporting** is allowed where turnover is quick, amounts large and
  maturities short (e.g. a bank's demand deposits), and for items held on behalf of
  customers.

<Callout kind="pointer">
This site presents **bank overdraft and cash credit movements as financing flows**
(the convention used throughout Ch 11's statements). Some presentations treat repayable-
on-demand overdrafts as a component of cash management — the SM's item list places
overdraft and cash credit with the financing borrowings, and consistency is what the
examiner rewards.
</Callout>

## 3. AS 17: Segment Reporting

One consolidated total hides the fact that a conglomerate's pharma arm grows while its
steel arm bleeds. AS 17 reports the parts. A **business segment** is a distinguishable
component providing an individual product/service or related group of them, **subject to
risks and returns different from other business segments**. A **geographical segment**
is the same idea for economic environments. The **primary** reporting format is whichever
of the two dominates the enterprise's risks and returns — usually visible from its
internal organisation and reporting structure; the other becomes **secondary** with
lighter disclosure.

A segment is **reportable** if it crosses **any one** of three 10% tests:

| Test | Threshold |
| --- | --- |
| Revenue | Segment revenue (external **plus inter-segment**) ≥ 10% of combined total revenue of all segments |
| Result | Segment result ≥ 10% of the **greater in absolute amount** of (i) combined result of all segments in profit, (ii) combined result of all segments in loss |
| Assets | Segment assets ≥ 10% of total segment assets |

Then the **75% floor**: if external revenue of the reportable segments is under 75% of
total enterprise revenue, keep adding segments (even sub-10% ones) until it isn't. A
segment reported last year stays reportable this year if management judges it of
continuing significance, even if it now fails all three tests.

Definitional hygiene the MCQs test: **segment revenue** excludes extraordinary items and
interest/dividend income (unless the segment's operations are primarily financial);
**segment expense** excludes interest expense (same caveat), extraordinary items, income
tax, and general head-office expenses. **Segment result = segment revenue − segment
expense** — pre-tax, pre-central-overhead. Primary-format disclosures: revenue (external
and inter-segment separately), result, assets, liabilities, capital expenditure,
depreciation, and other significant non-cash expenses.

<Callout kind="example">
Segments A/B/C/D earn results of ₹120, ₹(300), ₹40, ₹(20) lakh. Profits total ₹160;
losses total ₹(320). The 10% result test uses ₹320 (greater absolute) — a segment needs
|result| ≥ ₹32 lakh. A (₹120) and B (₹300) qualify on result; C (₹40) does too; D (₹20)
must look to the revenue or asset tests.
</Callout>

## 4. AS 18: Related Party Disclosures

An arm's-length market price is the silent assumption behind every number in the
accounts. Between related parties that assumption dies — a holding company can instruct
a subsidiary to sell at cost, or not to compete. AS 18 does not prohibit or re-price
anything; it makes the **relationship and the dealings visible**. Parties are related if
one **controls** or exercises **significant influence** over the other (or both are
under common control) **at any time during the year**.

The five related-party families:

1. Enterprises that directly or indirectly **control, or are controlled by, or are
   under common control** with the reporting enterprise (holding companies,
   subsidiaries, fellow subsidiaries).
2. **Associates and joint ventures**, and the investing parties/venturers in respect of
   which the reporting enterprise is an associate or JV.
3. **Individuals** owning voting power that gives control or significant influence,
   and **relatives** of any such individual.
4. **Key management personnel** and their relatives.
5. Enterprises over which any person in (3) or (4) can exercise significant influence.

**Control** = ownership (direct/indirect) of **more than half the voting power**, or
control of the **composition of the board**, or a **substantial interest in voting power
plus the power to direct financial/operating policies**. **Significant influence** =
participation in financial/operating policy decisions without control — presumed at
**20% or more of voting power**. A **relative** is the spouse, son, daughter, brother,
sister, father or mother who may be expected to influence, or be influenced by, the
individual.

Deemed **not** related: two companies merely sharing a **common director** (unless the
director can affect the policies of both in their mutual dealings); a **single customer,
supplier, franchiser, distributor or agent** on whom the enterprise merely depends
economically; **providers of finance, trade unions, public utilities and government
departments** in the ordinary course of their dealings.

Disclosure exemptions: where disclosure would **conflict with confidentiality duties
imposed by statute or a regulator**; **state-controlled enterprises** need not disclose
transactions with *other* state-controlled enterprises; and **intra-group transactions
eliminated on consolidation** need no disclosure in the consolidated statements.

What gets disclosed: where **control exists**, the name and nature of the relationship
**even if there were no transactions**. Where transactions occurred: the name, nature of
relationship, nature of the transactions and their **volume**, outstanding balances and
provisions for doubtful debts from related parties, and amounts written off or back.
Items of a similar nature may be **aggregated by type** unless separate disclosure is
needed to understand the effects. **Remuneration to key management personnel is itself a
related-party transaction** — a favourite trick stem.

## 5. AS 20: Earnings Per Share

Every P/E ratio quotes it, so AS 20 standardises it. Every company presents **basic and
diluted EPS on the face of the statement of profit and loss** — and an SMC's relief is
only from the *diluted* figure.

**Basic EPS** = net profit (or loss) **attributable to equity shareholders** ÷
**weighted average number of equity shares** outstanding. The numerator deducts
**preference dividends and any attributable tax** — for **cumulative** preference
shares, the full dividend for the period **whether or not declared**. The denominator
weights each block of shares by the fraction of the period it was outstanding, counted
from the **date consideration becomes receivable**. Partly paid shares count as a
**fraction of a share** to the extent they carry dividend rights during the period.

Issues that change share count **without a matching change in resources** are treated as
if they happened at the **beginning of the earliest period reported** — bonus issues and
share splits restate every period presented. A **rights issue below fair value** hides a
bonus element inside a full-price issue, so pre-rights share counts are multiplied by:

**Fair value per share immediately before the rights ÷ theoretical ex-rights value per
share (TERP)** — where TERP = (aggregate market value of shares before exercise +
rights proceeds) ÷ shares after exercise.

<Callout kind="example">
1,00,000 shares; fair value ₹25 cum-rights; 1-for-4 rights at ₹15. TERP =
(1,00,000×25 + 25,000×15) ÷ 1,25,000 = ₹23. Adjustment factor = 25/23. Prior-period and
pre-rights share counts are multiplied by 25/23; from the rights date, 1,25,000 actual
shares are weighted normally.
</Callout>

**Diluted EPS** asks: what if every **dilutive potential equity share** — convertible
debentures/preference shares, options, warrants, shares issuable on contingencies —
became equity? The numerator adds back the interest or preference dividend on the
dilutive instrument **net of tax**; the denominator adds the weighted shares issuable.
For **options**, only the *bonus* element dilutes: shares deemed issued for no
consideration = options × (1 − exercise price ÷ fair value). Potential shares are
**dilutive only if they reduce EPS from continuing ordinary activities**; anti-dilutive
series are ignored, and each series is tested separately, most dilutive first.

<Callout kind="mistake">
A loss year inverts intuition: converting a debenture *reduces the loss per share*,
which is anti-dilutive — so in a loss year most potential shares are **excluded** and
diluted EPS equals basic. Dilution means a *worse* figure for shareholders, not a
bigger denominator.
</Callout>

## 6. AS 24: Discontinuing Operations

When a company decides to exit a major line of business, the reader deserves to know
*before* the exit completes — the remaining business is what they are pricing. A
**discontinuing operation** is a **component** of an enterprise that:

- (a) the enterprise, **pursuant to a single plan**, is disposing of substantially in
  its entirety (single transaction, demerger, spin-off), disposing of piecemeal, or
  **terminating through abandonment**; and
- (b) represents a **separate major line of business or geographical area** of
  operations; and
- (c) can be **distinguished operationally and for financial reporting** purposes.

All three limbs must hold. Gradually phasing out a product line, shutting a few
branches, or shifting production between sites is **restructuring, not a discontinuing
operation**, because no separate major line is being exited under a single plan.

Disclosure starts at the **initial disclosure event** — the **earlier** of (i) entering
a **binding sale agreement** for substantially all the component's assets, and (ii) the
board **both approving a detailed formal plan and announcing it**. A detailed formal
plan pins down which major assets will go, the expected method and timing of the
disposal, the principal locations affected, the employees to be compensated, and the
estimated proceeds.

AS 24 sets **no new recognition or measurement rules** — provisions come from AS 29,
impairment from AS 28, employee terminations from AS 15. Its own demands are disclosure:
a description of the operation and the segment it sat in, the date and nature of the
initial disclosure event, the expected timing of completion, carrying amounts of assets
and liabilities to be disposed of, the **revenue, expenses and pre-tax profit or loss
attributable to the operation with its tax expense**, and its **net cash flows by
activity** — updated every period until the discontinuance is complete, with
**comparatives restated** to segregate continuing from discontinuing amounts.

<Callout kind="pointer">
A discontinuing operation is **not an extraordinary item** — its results stay inside
ordinary activities, separately disclosed. And once a plan is announced, abandoning the
plan is itself disclosable, with the reasons.
</Callout>

## 7. AS 25: Interim Financial Reporting

AS 25 **does not force anyone to report quarterly** — statute and regulators (SEBI's
listing obligations) decide who reports and how often. Its rule: **if** an enterprise is
required to, or elects to, present an interim financial report, it must comply. An
**interim period** is a reporting period **shorter than a full financial year**; an
interim report is either a **complete** set of statements or a **condensed** one.

Minimum contents of a condensed report: **condensed balance sheet, condensed statement
of profit and loss, condensed cash flow statement, and selected explanatory notes** —
each headline and subtotal from the latest annual statements survives condensation.
Selected notes cover what changed since the year end: policy changes, seasonality,
unusual items, estimate revisions, issues/buy-backs of securities, dividends, segment
data (if AS 17 applies), post-interim events, acquisitions and disposals.

The periods table the exam loves:

| Statement | Current | Comparative |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Balance sheet | As at the interim date | As at the **close of the preceding full financial year** |
| P&L | The interim period itself **plus** cumulative year-to-date | The matching interim period and matching year-to-date, one year back |
| Cash flow | Cumulative **year-to-date only** | The matching year-to-date, one year back |

Recognition and measurement: apply the **same accounting policies as the annual
statements**. Revenue that arrives **seasonally or occasionally is not anticipated or
deferred** at an interim date if such treatment would be improper at year end; costs
incurred **unevenly** are anticipated or deferred only if that treatment would also be
appropriate at the year end. **Materiality is assessed against the interim period's own
data**, not the expected annual figures.

Income tax is the calculation set-piece: interim tax expense uses the **best estimate of
the weighted average annual effective tax rate** expected for the full year, applied to
the interim period's pre-tax income, with later interim periods absorbing any change in
the estimate.

<Callout kind="example">
Estimated annual income ₹10,00,000; slab tax: 30% on the first ₹5,00,000 and 40% on the
rest → estimated annual tax ₹3,50,000 → weighted average annual effective rate 35%.
Quarterly pre-tax income of ₹2,50,000 bears tax of ₹87,500 each quarter — not 30% on
early quarters and 40% later.
</Callout>

If an estimate reported in an interim period **changes materially in the final interim
period** and no separate report is published for that period, the **nature and amount of
the change must be disclosed in the annual financial statements' notes**.

## 8. The chapter in one paragraph

AS 1 makes every enterprise disclose its significant accounting policies in one place,
flag departures from the three fundamental assumptions, and confess policy changes with
their money effect — while insisting disclosure never cures a wrong treatment. AS 3
re-sorts the year into operating, investing and financing cash flows around a
cash-equivalents core (≤ three months, insignificant risk), with interest/dividend
classification hinging on whether the enterprise is financial. AS 17 makes any segment
reportable that crosses 10% of revenue, of the greater-absolute profit-or-loss pool, or
of assets — topped up until reportable segments carry 75% of external revenue. AS 18
names five families of related parties around control (>50% votes / board / substantial
interest + policy power) and significant influence (20% presumption), then discloses
relationships, volumes and balances. AS 20 weights shares by time and consideration,
restates bonus issues retrospectively, splits a cheap rights issue into its bonus
element via fair-value/TERP, and admits only dilutive potential shares into diluted EPS.
AS 24 forces disclosure of a single-plan exit from a major line of business from the
earlier of a binding sale agreement or an approved-and-announced formal plan, using
other standards for all measurement. AS 25 disciplines whoever must (or opts to) report
interim: condensed statements, annual-policy consistency, and tax at the estimated
weighted average annual effective rate.
