A shop in Nagpur sells goods that a head office in Mumbai bought, priced and shipped. Who
keeps which books, and whose profit is it? Branch accounting answers that question three
times over, at three levels of branch independence — and then once more in a foreign
currency. The good news: each level has exactly one accounting idea at its centre, and the
exam questions recombine those ideas rather than invent new ones.

The map for this chapter: **dependent branches** (head office keeps the books — debtors
method, stock & debtors method, wholesale method), the **invoice-price loading** that runs
through all of them, **independent branches** (branch keeps its own books — current
accounts, transit items, incorporation), and **foreign branches** (translate first under
AS 11, then treat as an independent branch).

## 1. The branch map

A **branch** is any establishment carrying on the same business as the entity, away from the
principal place of business. How it is accounted for depends on who keeps its books:

| | Dependent branch | Independent branch |
|---|---|---|
| Books | Head office keeps them; branch keeps memoranda at most | Branch keeps full double-entry books |
| Central account | Branch Account (or Branch Stock/Debtors set) in HO books | Head Office Account in branch books, Branch Account in HO books — mirror images |
| Profit emerges in | HO books directly | Branch trial balance, later *incorporated* into HO books |
| Typical exam work | Loading adjustments, memorandum accounts | Reconciliation of the two current accounts, incorporation entries |

Do not confuse branch accounts with **departmental accounts**: departments sit under one
roof and one set of books, so the problem there is *allocating* shared expenses between
activities. Branches are geographically separate; the problem is *recording across
locations* — and for departments there is nothing like cash-in-transit or a foreign
currency translation.

A dependent branch typically sells for cash (sometimes credit), receives all goods from
head office, and remits takings to HO — petty cash aside, it holds no funds of its own.

## 2. Dependent branches: the debtors method

Under the **debtors method** the head office opens a single **Branch Account** and treats
the branch the way it would treat a customer. The account is *nominal in behaviour*: its
balancing figure is the branch profit or loss.

- **Debit** the Branch Account with what HO gives the branch: opening branch assets
  (stock, debtors, petty cash), goods sent to branch (net of returns), and branch expenses
  paid by HO (rent, salaries, insurance).
- **Credit** it with what comes back: remittances (cash sales banked + collections from
  debtors), and closing branch assets carried down.
- The gap is profit (credit side heavier) or loss.

Two habits keep this method honest when the branch also sells on credit:

<Callout kind="pointer">
  <p>
    <strong>Only money that actually moves belongs in the Branch Account.</strong> Credit
    sales, discounts allowed, and bad debts never enter it directly — they live in a
    <strong>memorandum Branch Debtors Account</strong> whose only exports to the Branch
    Account are the cash collected and the closing debtors balance. A memorandum Petty Cash
    Account plays the same role for expenses paid out of the float.
  </p>
</Callout>

The debtors method reports the result but not the anatomy — you cannot read gross profit,
shortages or pilferage from one all-purpose account. For control, larger branches use the
**stock & debtors method**: separate Branch Stock, Branch Debtors, Branch Expenses, Branch
Adjustment (where goods move at invoice price) and Branch Profit & Loss Accounts. The
Branch Stock Account, kept at selling/invoice price, exposes any **shortage or surplus**
as its balancing figure — the control the debtors method cannot give.

## 3. Invoice price and the loading

Head offices often invoice goods to a branch at **cost plus a fixed margin** (or at selling
price) — it hides the true profit margin from branch staff and polices the stock. The
add-on is the **loading**. Everything in this area is one conversion exercise:

- Invoice price = cost + 25% *of cost* → loading is 25/125 = **20% of invoice price**.
- Invoice price = cost + 1/3 of cost → loading is 1/4 of invoice price.
- In general: loading fraction of IP = margin ÷ (100 + margin).

Wherever a branch figure is stated at invoice price, the unrealised loading must be
stripped before profit is struck:

1. **Goods sent to branch** — the loading on the whole year's sendings is removed
   (Goods Sent to Branch A/c Dr → Branch Adjustment A/c, or deducted in the Branch A/c).
2. **Opening and closing stock** — a **Stock Reserve** equal to closing stock × loading
   fraction is carried; the opening reserve is written back. Net effect on profit:
   opening reserve − closing reserve.
3. **Shortages** found in the Branch Stock Account are split: the *loading part* cancels
   against the Branch Adjustment Account; only the *cost part* hits the Branch P&L (or
   goes to a shortage/pilferage account).

<Callout kind="mistake">
  <p>
    <strong>The classic slip is applying the margin to the wrong base.</strong> "Cost plus
    25%" and "25% on invoice price" are different fractions of the same rupees. Fix the
    base before touching the numbers: margin stated on cost → loading = margin/(100+margin)
    of IP; margin stated on selling price → use it directly.
  </p>
</Callout>

### Worked example 1 — debtors method at invoice price

Head office invoices goods at cost + 25%. During the year it sent goods invoiced at
₹5,00,000; branch opening stock ₹60,000 and closing stock ₹80,000 (both at invoice price);
remittances ₹5,20,000; branch expenses paid by HO ₹40,000; no debtors.

Loading = 25/125 = 1/5 of invoice price. Strip it everywhere it hides:

| Adjustment | Amount |
|---|---|
| Loading on goods sent (₹5,00,000 × 1/5) | ₹1,00,000 credit to Branch A/c effect |
| Stock reserve on closing stock (₹80,000 × 1/5) | ₹16,000 against this year |
| Stock reserve on opening stock (₹60,000 × 1/5) | ₹12,000 written back |

Branch A/c at invoice price: debits 60,000 + 5,00,000 + 40,000 = ₹6,00,000; credits
5,20,000 + 80,000 = ₹6,00,000 — apparently nil. Add the loading adjustments:
+1,00,000 − 16,000 + 12,000 = **profit ₹96,000**. The invoice-price veneer cancelled
itself; only the loading arithmetic produced the true result.

## 4. The wholesale branch method

Where a manufacturer sells both **wholesale to outsiders and retail through its own
branches**, the fair question is: how much of the branch's profit is really *retail*
skill, and how much would have been earned anyway by selling wholesale? The wholesale
method answers by invoicing branches **at the wholesale price**:

- **HO profit** = wholesale price − cost, on goods sent (earned even without the branch).
- **Branch profit** = retail sales − wholesale price of the goods actually sold.
- Branch closing stock stands at wholesale price, so HO carries a **stock reserve** equal
  to (wholesale − cost) on the branch's unsold stock; it is the unrealised slice of HO's
  own wholesale profit.

If cost is ₹100, wholesale ₹140, retail ₹160: on an item the branch has sold, HO books
₹40 and the branch books ₹20. On an item still on the branch shelf, the ₹40 sits in stock
at wholesale price and is neutralised by a ₹40 stock reserve until sold.

## 5. Independent branches and reconciliation

An independent branch keeps **complete double-entry books**, prepares its own trial
balance and final accounts, and records dealings with head office in a **Head Office
Account** — a proxy capital/current account. Head office mirrors it with a **Branch
Account**. In principle the two balances are equal and opposite; in practice, at the
period end, they disagree for reasons every exam recycles:

1. **Goods in transit** — HO debited Branch when dispatching; branch has not yet received.
2. **Cash in transit** — branch credited HO when remitting; HO has not yet received.
3. **HO expenses charged to the branch** (or depreciation — next point) entered by one
   side only.
4. **Branch fixed assets kept in HO books**: the asset account lives with HO, but
   depreciation is a branch expense — HO passes *Branch A/c Dr → To Branch Asset A/c*,
   the branch passes *Depreciation A/c Dr → To Head Office A/c*.
5. **Inter-branch transactions**, normally routed through head office as if each branch
   dealt with HO alone.

The transit items are adjusted **in the books of the head office** (the convention the SM
follows): *Goods in Transit A/c Dr → To Branch A/c* and *Cash in Transit A/c Dr → To
Branch A/c*, so both current accounts then agree. Transit items appear as assets in the
combined balance sheet — and in no one's stock.

### Worked example 2 — making the current accounts agree

HO's Branch Account shows a debit of ₹2,08,000; the branch's Head Office Account shows a
credit of ₹1,80,000. Investigation: goods invoiced at ₹20,000 were dispatched on 28 March
but received in April; the branch remitted ₹8,000 on 30 March, received by HO in April.

Work from the HO balance: ₹2,08,000 − goods in transit ₹20,000 − cash in transit ₹8,000 =
**₹1,80,000**, which matches the branch's book. Both entries are passed in HO books,
debiting the two transit accounts and crediting Branch Account by ₹28,000 in total.

**Incorporation.** To fold the branch's results into HO's final accounts, HO passes
incorporation entries from the branch trial balance: revenue items route through a
**Branch Trading and Profit & Loss Account** in HO books (the branch profit ends up as
*Branch A/c Dr → To Branch P&L*, or reversed for a loss), and branch assets and
liabilities are taken up item by item, closing the Branch Account. At the start of the
next year the incorporation of assets/liabilities is reversed if the branch continues to
keep its own books.

## 6. Foreign branches: AS 11 translation

A foreign branch keeps its books in its local currency; before its trial balance can be
incorporated, every figure must be **translated into Indian rupees under AS 11**. The
standard first asks what kind of operation the branch is:

- An **integral foreign operation** carries on business as an extension of the reporting
  enterprise — it sells goods imported from HO and remits the proceeds. Its activities'
  cash flows immediately affect HO.
- A **non-integral foreign operation** accumulates cash, incurs costs, raises finance and
  makes sales substantially **in its local currency** — a business with local autonomy;
  HO's exposure is to its *net investment*, not to each asset.

The translation rules follow the classification:

| Item | Integral (IFO) | Non-integral (NFO) |
|---|---|---|
| Monetary items (cash, debtors, creditors, loans) | Closing rate | Closing rate |
| Non-monetary items at historical cost (fixed assets, inventory at cost) | Rate at the date of the transaction (historical rate) | Closing rate |
| Income and expenses | Rate at transaction dates (average rate as approximation) | Rate at transaction dates (average rate as approximation) |
| Depreciation | Historical rate of the related asset | Closing rate (as for the asset) |
| Exchange difference | **Profit & loss account** | **Foreign Currency Translation Reserve (FCTR)**, until disposal of the net investment |

<Callout kind="pointer">
  <p>
    <strong>One line decides most MCQs here:</strong> integral → translate as if HO itself
    had made each transaction (mixed rates, difference to P&L); non-integral → translate
    the whole balance sheet at closing rate (income statement at average), park the
    difference in FCTR. On disposal of a non-integral operation the accumulated FCTR is
    recognised in the profit and loss account.
  </p>
</Callout>

When the classification itself changes: **integral → non-integral**, exchange differences
on non-monetary assets from the date of change go to FCTR; **non-integral → integral**,
the FCTR balance accumulated so far stays frozen until disposal, and the translated
amounts of non-monetary items at the change date become their historical costs.

### Worked example 3 — one trial balance, two answers

A New York branch shows: fixed assets $10,000 (bought when $1 = ₹62), debtors $4,000,
creditors $1,500, closing cash $500. Closing rate ₹83, average rate for the year ₹80.

*If integral:* fixed assets 10,000 × 62 = ₹6,20,000 (historical); debtors 4,000 × 83 =
₹3,32,000; creditors 1,500 × 83 = ₹1,24,500; cash 500 × 83 = ₹41,500 — monetary at
closing, non-monetary frozen at ₹62, and any resulting difference lands in P&L.

*If non-integral:* every one of those balance-sheet items uses ₹83 — fixed assets become
10,000 × 83 = ₹8,30,000 — and the difference goes to FCTR, not P&L. Same branch, same
year, ₹2,10,000 of asset value and the whole exchange effect classified differently.
That is why AS 11 makes you classify *before* you translate.

## 7. The whole toolkit, in one paragraph

Dependent branch: HO's books, Branch Account as quasi-debtor, memoranda for credit sales
and petty cash, stock & debtors set for control. Invoice pricing: convert the margin to a
fraction of invoice price and strip the loading from goods sent, both stocks, and
shortages. Wholesale method: branch earns only the retail-over-wholesale slice; stock
reserve at wholesale margin. Independent branch: mirror current accounts, transit items
adjusted in HO books, then incorporate. Foreign branch: classify under AS 11, translate
(mixed rates vs closing rate), difference to P&L or FCTR — then it is just an independent
branch in rupees.
