Both units of this chapter answer the same exam question — **when is revenue
earned, and how much of it belongs to this year?** — for two different kinds of
business. AS 7 serves the **contractor** whose flyover or dam straddles three
accounting years: it spreads contract revenue and contract costs over the
construction period by the **percentage of completion method**, and pulls every
expected loss forward the moment it is foreseen. AS 9 serves everyone else: it fixes
the **timing** of revenue from the **sale of goods** (property or risks-and-rewards
transferred), the **rendering of services** (proportionate completion or completed
service contract), and the **use by others of enterprise resources** — interest by
time, royalties by agreement, dividends by right.

Applicability shorthand (regime in
[Ch 3](/intermediate/advanced-accounting/applicability-of-accounting-standards/)):
**AS 7 and AS 9 bind every entity in full** — neither appears on any SMC or MSME
relief list.

## 1. AS 7: Construction Contracts

### Whose books, and why a special standard

A construction contract typically **takes more than one accounting year to
complete**, so its final outcome is known only years after work begins. Waiting for
completion would show cost-heavy loss years followed by one windfall year; AS 7
instead allocates revenue and cost to **the periods in which the work is
performed**. The standard governs accounting **in the financial statements of
contractors** — the party doing the construction; the customer is the
contractee. Two parties are essential: an engineering division building a machine
for its own production division is a **self-constructed asset under AS 10**, not an
AS 7 contract.

A **construction contract** is a contract **specifically negotiated** for the
construction of an asset — or a **combination of assets that are closely
interrelated or interdependent in terms of their design, technology and function
or their ultimate purpose or use** (a refinery, a complex plant). The standard also
covers two neighbours: contracts for **services directly related to the
construction** (project managers, architects), and contracts for **destruction or
restoration** of assets and of the environment following demolition.

### Segmenting and combining

The unit of account can be smaller or larger than the paper contract:

- **One contract, several assets — split it** when *all three* hold: **separate
  proposals** were submitted for each asset; each asset was **separately
  negotiated** and each part could be accepted or rejected; and each asset's
  **costs and revenues can be identified**.
- **Several contracts, one project — combine them** (single or several customers)
  when *all three* hold: negotiated as a **single package**; so closely
  interrelated they are in effect **one project with an overall profit margin**;
  and performed **concurrently or in a continuous sequence**.
- **An additional asset** ordered by the customer is a **separate contract** when
  *either* it **differs significantly in design, technology or function** from the
  original assets, *or* its **price is negotiated without regard to the original
  contract price**.

### Fixed price and cost-plus

In a **fixed price contract** the contractor agrees a fixed sum or a **fixed rate
per unit of output** — common in public tenders — sometimes with
**cost-escalation clauses**; escalation clauses do not change the category. In a
**cost-plus contract** the contractor is **reimbursed for allowable or otherwise
defined costs, plus a percentage of these costs or a fixed fee** — chosen when
scope or cost cannot be assessed up front.

### What goes into contract revenue

Contract revenue comprises the **initial amount agreed**, plus **variations,
claims and incentive payments** — but the add-ons count only **to the extent it is
probable they will result in revenue** and they are **capable of being reliably
measured**. In build-up form: agreed price, **plus** agreed cost escalation,
**plus** claims (cost reimbursements not in the contract price — only when
customer acceptance is probable), **plus** incentives (usually for early completion
— only when meeting the performance standard is probable), **less** penalties for
late completion, adjusted for variations.

**Worked example 1 — revenue build-up.** Dhavala Constructions signs a fixed-price
contract of ₹8 crore. The contract passes on material escalation to the extent of
25% of the increase in material cost, and passes on labour cost increases in full
provided the rise in minimum wages does not exceed 30%. During construction, material costs rise by
₹60,00,000 and labour costs rise 20% on an original estimate of ₹1,50,00,000. The
job finishes early, earning the agreed ₹40,00,000 incentive; a customer-approved
variation adds ₹75,00,000; and Dhavala has a probable, reliably measurable claim
of ₹15,00,000 for approval costs. Contract revenue = 800 + (25% × 60 = 15) +
(wage rise 20% is within the 30% ceiling, so the full increase passes:
1,50,00,000 × 20% = 30) + 40 + 75 + 15 =
**₹9,75,00,000**.

### What goes into contract costs

Three buckets: costs that **relate directly** to the specific contract; costs
**attributable to contract activity in general** that can be **allocated** to the
contract; and other costs **specifically chargeable to the customer** under the
contract.

- **Direct**: site labour including supervision, materials used, depreciation of
  plant used on the contract, moving plant and materials to and from the site,
  plant hire, contract-specific design, estimated **rectification and guarantee
  work including expected warranty costs**, third-party claims. Direct costs are
  **reduced by incidental income** that does not form part of contract revenue —
  say, selling off surplus materials, or disposing of plant once the contract ends.
- **Allocable**: insurance, design not tied to one contract, **construction
  overheads** — allocated on **normal levels of construction activity** — and
  borrowing costs per AS 16
  ([Ch 5](/intermediate/advanced-accounting/assets-based-as/)).
- **Excluded**: general administration and research and development for which
  **reimbursement is not specified** in the contract, **selling costs**, and
  **depreciation of idle plant** not used on a particular contract.

### The percentage of completion method

Because outcomes take years, AS 7 recognises revenue **in proportion to the stage
of completion** at each reporting date, matched with the costs of reaching that
stage — consistent with accrual and matching. The gate is a **reliable estimate of
the final outcome**. A **fixed price** contract's outcome is reliably estimable
when *all four* hold: total contract revenue is reliably measurable; inflow of
economic benefits is probable; **both** the costs to complete **and** the stage of
completion at the reporting date are reliably measurable; and contract costs are
clearly identifiable and reliably measurable so actuals can be **compared with
prior estimates**. A **cost-plus** contract needs only *two*: probable inflow, and
costs clearly identifiable and reliably measurable **whether or not specifically
reimbursable**.

**When the outcome cannot be estimated reliably**: recognise revenue **only to the
extent of contract costs incurred whose recovery is probable**, and expense
contract costs **in the period incurred** — zero profit, but not necessarily zero
revenue. Once the uncertainty lifts, switch to percentage of completion.

**Expected losses wait for nothing.** Whenever total contract costs are expected
to exceed total contract revenue, the **entire expected loss is recognised as an
expense immediately** — whatever the stage of completion, under prudence.

### Measuring the stage of completion

Use the method that **measures reliably the work performed**: the proportion that
**contract costs incurred for work performed to date** bear to **estimated total
contract costs**; or **surveys of work performed** (surveyor certification, common
in government projects); or **completion of a physical proportion** of the work
(tiles laid over area to be tiled). **Progress payments and advances received from
customers may not reflect the work performed** — cash collected is not a stage
measure.

Two refinements to the cost basis: costs that **relate to future activity** —
materials delivered to site but not yet used — are excluded from costs-to-date and
carried as an **asset (contract work in progress)** if recovery is probable; and
when an amount **already recognised** as contract revenue turns doubtful of
collection, it is written off as an **expense** — never as an adjustment of
contract revenue.

**Worked example 2 — a loss-making contract, year 1.** Kaveri Infra has a fixed
price contract of ₹900 lakh. By the year end it has incurred ₹420 lakh (of which
₹20 lakh is material stored at site for next year's work) and estimates a further
₹600 lakh to complete — an estimate that already counts consuming the ₹20 lakh of
materials stored at site. Total estimated cost = 400 + 600 = ₹1,000 lakh; stage of
completion = 400/1,000 = **40%**; revenue = 900 × 40% = **₹360 lakh**. The
foreseeable loss is 1,000 − 900 = **₹100 lakh** and is recognised **now**: the
current year's P&L already shows 360 − 400 = ₹40 lakh of it, so a **provision of
₹60 lakh** tops the loss up to ₹100 lakh.

### Changes in estimates

Percentage of completion is applied **on a cumulative basis** to the **current**
estimates of revenue and costs each period. A revision of either — or of the
outcome — is a **change in accounting estimate under AS 5**: it reshapes the
current and future periods, and no prior period is restated.

### Disclosure

Three general disclosures: contract revenue recognised in the period, the
**methods used to determine that revenue**, and the **methods used to determine
the stage of completion** of contracts in progress. For **contracts in progress**
at the reporting date: aggregate **costs incurred plus recognised profits (less
recognised losses)** to date, **advances received**, and **retentions**. Know the
three definitions: **retentions** are progress billings unpaid until contract
conditions are satisfied or defects rectified; **progress billings** are amounts
billed for work performed **whether or not paid**; **advances** are amounts
received **before** the related work is performed.

Finally, present the net position per contract: costs incurred **plus** recognised
profits **less** recognised losses **less** progress billings. Positive → **gross
amount due from customers** (asset); negative → **gross amount due to customers**
(liability).

<Callout kind="mistake">
Billing is not earning. Progress billings and advances drive neither revenue nor
stage of completion — a contractor 40% complete that has billed 60% still books
40% of revenue, and the excess billing sits in "gross amount due to customers".
The same discipline applies to the loss rule: a provision for the **whole**
expected loss cannot wait for the completion percentage to catch up.
</Callout>

## 2. AS 9: Revenue Recognition

### What counts as revenue

**Revenue** is the **gross inflow of cash, receivables or other consideration
arising in the course of the ordinary activities** of an enterprise from the
**sale of goods**, the **rendering of services**, and the **use by others of
enterprise resources yielding interest, royalties and dividends**. AS 9 is
mandatory for all enterprises, but four streams with special considerations stay
out: **construction contracts** (AS 7), **hire-purchase and lease agreements**,
**government grants** (AS 12), and **revenue of insurance companies from insurance
contracts**.

"Ordinary activities" does the sorting. A real-estate dealer selling a plot of
land books the **entire sale price as revenue** — land is its inventory; a
manufacturer selling a used factory machine books **no revenue at all**, only a
gain or loss on disposal. Also outside the definition: realised gains on disposal
and unrealised gains on holding **non-current assets**; unrealised holding gains
on current assets and natural increases in herds and forest products; realised or
unrealised **foreign exchange gains**; gains from discharging an obligation below
its carrying amount; and gains from restating the carrying amount of an
obligation.

### Agents earn commission, principals earn the price

In an **agency relationship**, revenue is **the commission** — not the gross
inflow. The test is **who bears the risks and rewards** of the goods or services:
an entity that must provide the goods itself is a **principal**; one that merely
**arranges** for another party to provide them is an **agent**. A food-delivery
platform that collects ₹300 (₹200 food + ₹60 delivery + ₹40 GST) recognises only
its ₹60 delivery charge — the restaurant recognises ₹200, and GST is nobody's
revenue: it is a **liability payable to government**. But a ticket reseller that
**buys 100 airline tickets and bears the loss on unsold ones** has taken the risks
— it is a principal and books the gross ticket sales.

### Sale of goods: the performance test

Performance is achieved — and revenue is recognised, **provided at the time of
performance it is not unreasonable to expect ultimate collection** — when all of
this holds:

1. the seller has **transferred to the buyer the property in the goods** for a
   price, **or** all **significant risks and rewards of ownership** have been
   transferred and the seller **retains no effective control** of the goods to a
   degree usually associated with ownership; **and**
2. **no significant uncertainty exists** regarding the amount of the
   **consideration** that will be derived from the sale.

Property transfer usually coincides with risk-and-reward transfer; where they
diverge (delivery delayed through a party's fault, risk contractually passing at a
different time), revenue follows **the risks and rewards**. A seller who ships
goods but keeps the transit insurance in its own favour, with legal title passing
on arrival, has kept the risks — revenue waits for the goods to **reach the
buyer**.

### Timing in the classic scenarios

- **Delivery delayed at the buyer's request** (bill and hold): the sale is
  complete — even with goods physically in the seller's warehouse — when the goods
  are **specifically identified** and not usable for another customer, the delay
  is **at the buyer's request**, and the goods are **ready for delivery**.
- **Sale on approval**: no revenue until the buyer **formally accepts**, does an
  **act adopting the transaction**, or the **rejection period lapses** (or a
  reasonable time, where none is fixed).
- **Goods subject to installation**: if installation is **complex and significant**
  to using the goods, wait for its satisfactory completion; if it is **simple**
  (plug in the refrigerator), recognise on the customer's agreement to purchase.
  A separate **installation fee** is service revenue — recognised when the
  installation is complete.
- **Sale and repurchase agreements**: a sale with an agreement to buy the goods
  back is a **financing arrangement** — recognise a **borrowing**, not a sale; the
  excess of the repurchase price over the sale price is **interest expense**.
- **Consignment sales**: the consignee holds the goods on the consignor's behalf —
  **no revenue until the goods are sold to a third party**; unsold consignment
  stock is the consignor's **closing inventory**.
- **Trade discounts and volume rebates** are **not revenue at all** — deduct them
  in determining revenue. **Cash discounts are different**: revenue stays at the
  gross amount, and the discount is an **expense** when the customer pays net.

### Rendering of services

Revenue is usually recognised **as the service is performed**, by one of two
methods. The **proportionate completion method** fits performance consisting of
**more than one act** — revenue follows each act performed. The **completed
service contract method** fits performance that is a **single act** (installing a
machine, a repair), or where the unperformed acts are **so significant** that
performance is not complete until they happen — revenue waits for the **sole or
final act** that makes the service chargeable. The same collection proviso
applies: postpone if ultimate collection cannot reasonably be expected at the
time of performance.

### Interest, royalties and dividends

Recognise only when **no significant uncertainty as to measurability or
collectability exists**, on three distinct bases: **interest** on a **time
proportion basis** (amount outstanding × applicable rate); **royalties** on an
**accrual basis per the terms of the agreement**; **dividends** when the
**owner's right to receive payment is established** — for a proposed dividend,
that is the date of **declaration**, not the proposal date or the year the units
were held.

### Uncertainty about collection: two different answers

Timing decides the treatment. If collectability is **already doubtful at the time
of raising the claim** — price escalations, export incentives, penal interest with
a poor recovery record — **postpone recognition** to the extent of the
uncertainty, and recognise the amount **in the period it becomes reasonably
certain** (that later receipt is *not* a prior period item, and per AS 5 it is not
extraordinary either — only size/nature disclosure may be needed). If the
uncertainty **arises after** the sale or service was recognised, do **not** adjust
revenue — make a **separate provision** (or write off a bad debt). Where there is
no uncertainty, instalment payment terms alone never delay recognition.

**Worked example 3 — one year, four transactions.** Sindhu Traders reports: (i)
goods of ₹80,000 sold on 25 March, held in its warehouse at the buyer's request,
identified and ready — **recognise ₹80,000**; (ii) goods of ₹2,00,000 sent on
consignment, of which 30% remain unsold with the consignee — **recognise
₹1,40,000**, the rest is Sindhu's inventory; (iii) goods of ₹1,00,000 sold on
three-month approval on 1 February, with approval received for 60% by 31 March
and silence on the rest — **recognise ₹60,000**, the balance waits for approval or
1 May; (iv) gross cash sales of ₹5,00,000 carrying a 4% trade discount —
**recognise ₹4,80,000**. Total revenue = **₹7,60,000**.

<Callout kind="pointer">
Exam sorting rule: the moment the seller is a *contractor building over years*,
leave AS 9 — contract revenue, expected losses and stage of completion are AS 7
territory. Within AS 9, most marks ride on four hinges: risks-and-rewards (not
delivery, not cash) for goods; commission-only for agents; the three bases for
interest/royalties/dividends; and postpone-vs-provision for collection
uncertainty depending on when the doubt arose.
</Callout>

## 3. The chapter in one paragraph

AS 7 makes the contractor recognise contract revenue (agreed price plus reliably
measurable, probable escalations, claims and incentives, less penalties) and
contract costs (direct plus allocable, never selling, unspecified admin/R&D or
idle-plant depreciation) by the percentage of completion method — cost proportion,
survey, or physical proportion, never progress billings — once the outcome is
reliably estimable (four conditions fixed-price, two cost-plus); if it is not,
revenue is capped at recoverable costs incurred; and an expected loss is expensed
in full immediately, with estimate revisions handled prospectively under AS 5 and
the net contract position disclosed as gross amounts due from/to customers. AS 9
recognises revenue — the gross inflow from ordinary activities — on sale of goods
when property or significant risks and rewards pass without significant
consideration uncertainty (bill-and-hold on three conditions, approval sales on
acceptance or lapse, complex installations on completion, sale-and-repurchase as
borrowing, consignment on onward sale, trade discounts deducted but cash discounts
expensed); on services by proportionate completion or completed service contract;
and on resources lent to others by time proportion (interest), agreement terms
(royalties) and establishment of the right (dividends) — postponing recognition
when collection is doubtful at claim time, and providing separately when doubt
arrives later.
