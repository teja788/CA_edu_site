The liability side of the balance sheet gets its two hardest judgement calls in this
chapter. AS 15 asks: **what does an employer owe its people** for service already
rendered — salaries and leave, bonuses, gratuity and pension, golden handshakes — and
insists the cost lands in the years the service is given, not the year the cash leaves.
AS 29 asks the broader question: **when does an uncertain obligation become real enough
to book**? Its answer — a present obligation from a past event, a probable outflow, and
a reliable estimate — draws the line between a **provision** (recognise), a
**contingent liability** (disclose) and background noise (do nothing), and it exists
mainly to stop enterprises from parking profits in fat years inside rainy-day
provisions and releasing them in lean ones.

Applicability shorthand (the regime lives in
[Ch 3](/intermediate/advanced-accounting/applicability-of-accounting-standards/)):
both standards bind every entity, but each carries relaxations. AS 15: SMCs and MSMEs
may skip the recognition and measurement rules for **non-vesting accumulating
short-term absences** and get discounting/disclosure relief — an SMC running a defined
benefit plan must **still do the actuarial valuation by the Projected Unit Credit
Method**, unless its average number of employees is **below 50**, in which case AS 15
lets it estimate the accrued liability by another rational method; a non-company
**MSME may use any rational method** regardless of size. AS 29:
SMCs and MSMEs are exempt from the **class-wise provision-movement disclosures**
(paragraphs 66–67); the recognition rules apply to everyone.

## 1. AS 15: Employee Benefits

### What the standard covers

AS 15 deals with **every form of consideration an enterprise gives in exchange for
service rendered by employees** — with one carve-out (employee **share-based
payments**, which follow the ICAI Guidance Note instead) and one boundary (the standard
governs the **employer's** books, not the reporting of the benefit plans themselves).

Four opening clarifications set its width. The benefits reach **all kinds of
employees** — full-time, part-time, permanent, casual or temporary — and the standard
expressly pulls in **whole-time directors and other management personnel**. Benefits
may be settled **in cash or in kind**. They include amounts for the employee's
**dependents** — spouse, children and others. And payment may flow to **anyone** — the
employee, a legal heir or nominee, an insurer or a trust.

The standard never defines "employee", so substance decides. A **contract *of*
service** (obey orders as to the work, its mode and manner) points to employment; a
**contract *for* services** (professional or technical work under limited direction)
points away — which is why outsourcing arrangements generally sit outside AS 15,
subject always to a careful reading. Helpful indicators of an employment relationship:
treatment as an employee for legal, tax or social-security purposes, heavy oversight
with employer-provided tools, work at the employer's premises, service through an
entity that has no other clients, faces little financial risk, or needs the employer's
permission to work elsewhere.

Obligations need not be written anywhere. Benefits arise from formal plans and
agreements, from legislation — and from **informal practices that leave the enterprise
no realistic alternative but to pay** (a consistently granted festival payment, a
predictable cost-of-living increase). A pattern that can be reliably projected creates
an obligation; a one-off concession granted only through collective bargaining does
not.

Everything the standard covers falls into **four families**, each with its own
recognition machinery:

1. **Short-term benefits** — wages, salaries, paid annual and sick leave,
   profit-sharing and bonuses payable within twelve months of the year end,
   non-monetary perks (medical care, housing, cars) for current employees.
2. **Post-employment benefits** — gratuity, pension, provident fund, post-employment
   medical care.
3. **Other long-term benefits** — sabbatical and long-service leave, jubilee awards,
   long-term disability benefits, bonuses and deferred pay not wholly payable within
   twelve months of the year end.
4. **Termination benefits** — payments for ending employment early, VRS packages
   included.

### Short-term employee benefits

Short-term benefits (other than termination benefits) **fall due wholly within twelve
months after the end of the period** in which the service is rendered. Their
accounting needs no actuarial assumptions and no discounting: recognise the
**undiscounted** amount attributable to service already rendered as an **expense**
(unless another standard lets it ride into an asset's cost), and carry any gap between
expense and cash as a **liability** (unpaid) or a **prepayment** (paid ahead).

<Callout kind="example">
Salary cost for the year is ₹2.40 crore; ₹2.25 crore is paid by 31 March and the rest
in April. The full ₹2.40 crore is the year's expense; the unpaid ₹15 lakh sits as a
liability at the year end. Timing of payment never moves the expense.
</Callout>

**Compensated absences** split by behaviour. **Accumulating** absences carry forward
for future use — **vesting** if unused entitlement is encashable on leaving,
**non-vesting** if it lapses with departure. Either way the obligation builds as the
service is rendered, so the enterprise recognises the expected cost as employees earn
the entitlement, measured as the **additional amount expected to be paid because of
the unused entitlement standing at the balance sheet date**. Vesting and non-vesting
follow the same recognition rule; for non-vesting leave the measurement simply factors
in the chance that employees leave before using it. **Non-accumulating** absences
(maternity leave, typically casual leave) neither carry forward nor link to past
service — no liability builds, and the cost hits the P&L **when the absence occurs**.

<Callout kind="example">
Sixty employees each earn eight working days of leave a year, usable one year forward.
Past experience says 54 of them will stay within their fresh entitlement next year and
six will overshoot by an average of two days each, drawing on the carried balance. The
liability at the year end is **12 days of pay** — the extra amount the enterprise
expects to pay only because unused entitlement has accumulated.
</Callout>

Whether carried-forward **earned leave** is short-term or long-term turns on two
readings together: the benefit must have **fallen due** (the employee can encash or
avail it within the next twelve months) *and* be **expected to be utilised** in that
window. Where the rules cap what can be taken — say 100 days stand to credit but only
30 may be encashed or availed in the coming year — or where behaviour shows the pile
will not clear in a year, the benefit is **long-term**, measured under the
other-long-term rules instead (and then it *is* discounted). The call is made on the
**overall behavioural pattern of all employees**, never employee by employee.

**Profit-sharing and bonus plans** are recognised when — and only when — the
enterprise has a **present obligation** to pay as a result of past events *and* a
**reliable estimate** can be made, which in practice demands a **formula** in the
plan. Measure what is actually expected to flow: a plan promising 3% of net profit to
employees who serve the full year is booked at, say, 2.6% once expected staff turnover
is counted.

### Post-employment benefits: two families

Every post-employment plan is one of two species, and the split drives everything:

- **Defined contribution plans (DCP)** — the employer pays **fixed contributions** to
  a separate fund and owes nothing more. **Actuarial risk** (benefits smaller than
  hoped) and **investment risk** (the fund earns less than needed) sit **on the
  employee**. Provident fund is the standing example. Accounting is a formality: the
  period's contribution goes straight to expense.
- **Defined benefit plans (DBP)** — everything else. The employer has promised an
  outcome, so **both risks stay on the employer**, and the charge comes from a full
  actuarial computation. Gratuity is the classic case.

Plans run by several employers, plans established by legislation and run by the state,
and insured plans all resolve into one of the two species by substance. **Paying
insurance premiums does not by itself buy an exit**: an insured plan is treated as
defined contribution **unless** the enterprise would have to (a) **pay the benefits
directly** when they fall due or (b) **top up** whatever the insurer fails to pay —
either residual obligation makes it a defined benefit plan.

One presentation nuance: fund balances that qualify as **plan assets** are *netted*
against the obligation, so the balance sheet shows only the net figure;
**reimbursement rights** (both measured at fair value) are shown **gross** — liability
on one side, a separate asset on the other — swelling both sides of the balance sheet.

### Defined benefit accounting

The **balance-sheet liability** is a net of three pieces:

> present value of the defined benefit obligation
> **minus** past service cost not yet recognised
> **minus** fair value of plan assets out of which the obligation is to be settled.

If plan assets are rich enough, the net turns negative — an asset. The asset is then
capped at the **lower of** the amount computed and the **present value of economic
benefits available as refunds from the plan or reductions in future contributions**
(the asset ceiling).

**Worked example 1 — net liability, then the ceiling.** PV of obligation ₹12,40,000;
past service cost not yet recognised ₹40,000; plan assets at fair value ₹9,00,000.
Liability = 12,40,000 − 40,000 − 9,00,000 = **₹3,00,000**. Now suppose instead the plan
assets stood at ₹13,60,000: the computation gives a negative ₹1,60,000, but if refunds
and contribution holidays are worth only ₹1,00,000 in present value, the balance sheet
shows an asset of **₹1,00,000**, not ₹1,60,000.

The **P&L charge** stitches together **seven components**: current service cost;
interest cost on the obligation; **expected** return on plan assets (and on
reimbursement rights); actuarial gains and losses recognised; past service cost
recognised; the effect of curtailments and settlements; and the effect of the asset
ceiling.

**Actuarial assumptions** must be **unbiased and mutually compatible** — best
estimates, neither imprudent nor excessively conservative, respecting the economic
links between inflation, salary growth, asset returns and discount rates. They come in
two boxes: **demographic** (mortality in and after service, turnover, disability,
early retirement, dependants eligible, medical claim rates) and **financial** (the
discount rate, future salary and benefit levels, future medical costs, expected return
on plan assets). Financial assumptions ride on **market expectations at the balance
sheet date** for the whole settlement horizon, stated in nominal terms unless
index-linked benefits make real terms more reliable.

**Actuarial gains and losses** — experience differences plus the effect of changed
assumptions — are recognised **immediately in the statement of profit and loss**. No
spreading, no corridor.

<Callout kind="mistake">
An actuarial surplus may not be smoothed over future years by trimming contributions
in the books — the whole gain is P&L income of the year it arises. (Contrast Ind AS
19, which parks remeasurements in OCI; AS 15 has no such route.)
</Callout>

**Worked example 2 — expected vs actual return on plan assets.** Plan assets open the
year at ₹3,00,000 (expected return 10% a year, taken as 5% for a half-year). On
30 September the plan pays out benefits of ₹30,000 and receives contributions of
₹70,000; assets close at ₹3,80,000. Expected return = 10% on 3,00,000 held all year
(30,000) + 5% on the net ₹40,000 inflow held six months (2,000) = **₹32,000**. Actual
return = closing 3,80,000 − opening 3,00,000 − contributions 70,000 + benefits paid
30,000 = **₹40,000**. The ₹8,000 excess of actual over expected is an actuarial gain
on plan assets, recognised at once.

A **settlement** wipes out further obligations for some or all plan benefits (a
commuted pension); a **curtailment** shrinks the plan — fewer employees covered, or
benefits cut (a segment shut under a union agreement where its people earn no further
benefit). Gains and losses land **when the settlement or curtailment occurs**.

**Worked example 3 — curtailment.** Gross obligation ₹9,00,000; plan assets
₹6,50,000; unamortised past service cost ₹60,000. A curtailment trims the obligation
by 15%. Gain = reduction 1,35,000 − 15% of the past service cost (9,000) =
**₹1,26,000**. The liability then on the books: reduced obligation 7,65,000 − assets
6,50,000 − remaining past service cost 51,000 = **₹64,000**.

### Other long-term and termination benefits

**Other long-term benefits** — sabbatical and long-service leave, jubilee awards,
long-term disability benefits, profit-sharing, bonuses and deferred compensation
payable twelve months or more after the period end — use a simplified version of the
defined benefit machinery, with changes recognised immediately.

**Termination benefits** are triggered by the employer's decision to end employment
early or the employee's acceptance of **voluntary redundancy** (VRS). Recognise a
liability and expense **only when** the enterprise has a **detailed formal plan, duly
approved**, *and* a **reliable estimate** of the cost — the standard-setters wanted
more than paperwork, e.g. employees actually signing up, before the liability counts
as crystallised. Benefits falling due **within twelve months** of the balance sheet
date go in **undiscounted**; later ones are **discounted**. Where the offer is open,
measure by the **number of employees expected to accept**; while acceptance is
uncertain, a **contingent liability** exists, disclosed under AS 29. Large termination
charges may need AS 5 disclosure (size, nature, incidence), and packages for key
management personnel fall under AS 18.

## 2. AS 29: Provisions, Contingent Liabilities and Contingent Assets

### Why the standard exists

Left alone, an enterprise with a fat year could invent a "provision", depress the
profit, and quietly write it back in a thin year — **profit smoothing**. AS 29 makes
provisioning a matter of criteria, not mood: recognition needs defined conditions,
measurement needs a best estimate the auditor can test, and disclosure lets a reader
see every provision move. The standard applies to accounting periods commencing on or
after 1 April 2004.

### Scope

AS 29 governs all provisions, contingent liabilities and contingent assets **except**
four kinds: those from **financial instruments carried at fair value**; those from
**executory contracts** — contracts where neither side has performed, or both have
performed equally — **unless the contract is onerous**; an **insurer's obligations
under contracts with its policy-holders**; and those **covered by another
standard** — AS 7 construction contracts, AS 9 revenue, AS 15 employee benefits, AS 19
leases, AS 22 taxes each take their own.

Note the word: *provision* here means a liability of uncertain timing or amount —
depreciation, doubtful debts and impairment are "provisions" only in name; they are
adjustments to asset carrying amounts and live in other standards.

### The vocabulary

- A **provision** is a liability which can be measured only by using a substantial
  degree of estimation.
- A **liability** is a present obligation of the enterprise arising from past events,
  the settlement of which is expected to result in an outflow from the enterprise of
  resources embodying economic benefits.
- An **obligating event** is an event that creates an obligation that results in an
  enterprise having no realistic alternative to settling that obligation.
- A **contingent liability** is **either** (a) a *possible* obligation from past
  events, whose existence will be confirmed only by the occurrence or non-occurrence
  of one or more uncertain future events not wholly within the enterprise's control,
  **or** (b) a *present* obligation that fails recognition — because the outflow is
  not probable, or (rarely) because no reliable estimate can be made.
- A **contingent asset** is a possible asset arising from past events whose existence
  will be confirmed only by uncertain future events not wholly within the
  enterprise's control.
- An obligation is a **present obligation** if, on the evidence, its existence at the
  balance sheet date is **more likely than not**; it is a **possible obligation** if
  its existence is considered *not* probable.
- A **restructuring** is a programme planned and controlled by management that
  materially changes either the scope of a business or the manner in which it is
  conducted.

### Recognition: the triple test

Recognise a provision **when all three hold**:

1. a **present obligation** exists as a result of a **past event**;
2. an **outflow of resources embodying economic benefits** to settle it is
   **probable** — more likely than not; and
3. a **reliable estimate** of the amount can be made.

Fail any limb and no provision is recognised. The whole decision collapses into one
table:

| Situation | Treatment |
|---|---|
| Present obligation, probable outflow, reliable estimate | **Provision** + disclosures |
| Possible obligation, or present obligation with outflow not probable | **Contingent liability disclosed** |
| Outflow remote | **Nothing** — no provision, no disclosure |
| Present obligation, no reliable estimate (extremely rare) | Disclose as contingent liability |

**Present obligation.** Weigh all evidence — expert opinions included, and events
after the balance sheet date. More likely than not that the obligation exists →
provision territory; otherwise → disclosure, unless remote. A warranty given on sale
creates a present obligation the day the product is sold.

**Past event.** Financial statements picture the position **at** the balance sheet
date, not the business's possible futures — so no provision for costs of operating in
years to come. Only obligations that exist **independently of the enterprise's future
actions** qualify. Penalties and clean-up costs for **unlawful environmental damage**
qualify: the outflow follows whatever the enterprise does next. An obligation to fit
**smoke filters** by a future date does not — the enterprise could change how it
operates; there is no present obligation even for an overdue retrofit (the fine that
non-compliance may attract is assessed separately). Decommissioning an oil rig is
provided **to the extent of damage already caused**. And where the obligation waits on
a **new law**, it arises only when enactment is **virtually certain** — until then,
draft legislation obliges nobody.

**Probable outflow.** Judge single obligations on their own facts; judge **a class of
similar obligations** (product warranties) as a whole — each individual claim may be
unlikely, yet some outflow across the class is probable, and that is enough.

**Reliable estimate.** Estimation is the ordinary stuff of financial statements, and
AS 29 expects a usable range in all but **extremely rare** cases. Where genuinely no
reliable estimate can be made, the liability exists but cannot be recognised — it is
disclosed as a contingent liability.

### Contingent liabilities and contingent assets

A **contingent liability is never recognised** — it is **disclosed**, unless the
possibility of outflow is **remote**. Where an enterprise is **jointly and severally
liable**, the slice expected to be met by the other parties is a contingent
liability; its own probable slice is provided. Contingent liabilities are
**reassessed continually**: the day the outflow turns probable, a provision is
recognised in that period's statements.

A **contingent asset is never recognised** either — booking it could recognise income
that never arrives. It is **not even disclosed in the financial statements**: if the
inflow is probable, it is described in the **report of the approving authority** (the
Board's report, for a company). When realisation becomes **virtually certain**, the
asset stops being contingent and is recognised in that period.

<Callout kind="mistake">
"Disclose the contingent asset in the notes" is a trap the exam sets on purpose —
under AS 29 the notes to the financial statements carry contingent *liabilities*;
contingent *assets* appear only in the approving authority's report, and only when
inflow is probable.
</Callout>

### Measurement

The amount is the **best estimate of the expenditure required to settle the present
obligation at the balance sheet date** — management's judgement, seasoned by
experience of similar transactions and, where needed, independent expert reports.
Measure **before tax** (tax consequences belong to AS 22). Build in **risks and
uncertainties** — but do not double-count by inflating both the cash flows and a
prudence cushion.

**No discounting** — with one carved-in exception: **decommissioning, restoration and
similar liabilities that are recognised as part of the cost of property, plant and
equipment** *are* discounted, at a **pre-tax rate reflecting current market
assessments of the time value of money and the risks specific to the liability** (not
risks already netted from the cash flows), and the periodic **unwinding of the
discount goes to the P&L**.

**Future events** count only with **sufficient objective evidence**: expected cost
reductions from applying *existing* technology at scale may be reflected; a hoped-for
brand-new clean-up technology may not. New legislation is factored in only when
virtually certain to be enacted and implemented. **Gains on expected disposal of
assets are never netted** against a provision, however closely linked — they wait for
the disposal, under the standard that governs the asset.

### Reimbursements

Where an insurer, an indemnity clause or a supplier warranty will refund part of the
outlay, the refund is a **separate asset**, recognised **only when receipt is
virtually certain** upon settlement — and **never above the provision** it offsets.
The P&L may present the expense **net** of the reimbursement, but the balance sheet
shows the gross provision and the asset separately. If the enterprise would not have
to pay were the third party to default, there is no liability for that slice at all —
keep it out of the provision. If reimbursement is expected but not virtually certain,
it is merely disclosed.

### Housekeeping: review, reversal, use

Provisions are **reviewed at each balance sheet date** and adjusted to the current
best estimate; the moment the outflow stops being probable, the provision is
**reversed**. And a provision may be **used only for the expenditure it was created
for** — burying a different cost inside an old provision conceals two events at once.

**Worked example 4 — a warranty provision rolls forward.** Sales carry a two-year
warranty, provided at 2% of invoice value where less than a year of warranty remains
and 3% where more than a year remains. Invoices: ₹1,00,000 (1 March 20X0), ₹60,000
(15 November 20X0), ₹2,00,000 (10 August 20X1). At 31 March 20X1 the first invoice has
under a year of cover left, the second more than a year: provision = 2,000 + 1,800 =
**₹3,800**. At 31 March 20X2 the first invoice's warranty has expired, the second has
under a year left, the third more than a year: provision = 1,200 + 6,000 = **₹7,200**;
the P&L charge for 20X1–X2 is 7,200 − 3,800 = **₹3,400**.

### Applications the exam loves

**Future operating losses** meet neither the definition of a liability nor the
recognition criteria — **never provided**. (Expected losses right up to a
restructuring date are still future operating losses — excluded.)

**Restructuring** — selling or terminating a line of business, closing or relocating
locations, stripping out a management layer, fundamental reorganisations — is
provided only when the general triple test is met. For a **sale of an operation**,
no obligation arises until there is a **binding sale agreement**: until signature the
enterprise can change its mind. A restructuring provision carries **only direct
expenditures** that are both **necessarily entailed by the restructuring** and **not
associated with continuing activities**. Out, therefore: **retraining or relocating
continuing staff, marketing, investment in new systems and distribution networks** —
those serve the future business. Gains on expected asset disposals stay out, however
integral to the plan.

### Disclosure

For **each class of provision**: opening and closing carrying amounts, additions
(including increases to existing provisions), amounts **used**, and unused amounts
**reversed** — plus the nature and expected timing of the outflows, uncertainty
indicators (with major assumptions where needed), and any expected reimbursement with
the asset recognised for it. **SMCs and MSMEs are exempt from these paragraphs.** For
each class of **contingent liability** (unless remote): nature, and where practicable
an estimate of the financial effect, the uncertainties, and the possibility of
reimbursement — stating the fact where any of this is impracticable. In **extremely
rare** cases where disclosure would **seriously prejudice** the enterprise in a
dispute, the detail may be withheld; the enterprise then discloses the general nature
of the dispute and the fact of, and reason for, the omission.

## 3. The chapter in one paragraph

AS 15 sorts everything an employer owes its people into four families — short-term
(undiscounted expense as service is rendered, with accumulating absences and
formula-based bonuses provided ahead of payment), post-employment (defined
contribution = pay and forget; defined benefit = full actuarial machinery: net
liability of obligation minus unrecognised past service cost minus plan assets, seven
P&L components, market-based mutually compatible assumptions, actuarial gains and
losses straight to P&L, curtailments and settlements when they occur), other
long-term (same machinery, simplified), and termination benefits (approved detailed
plan plus reliable estimate; discount beyond twelve months). AS 29 turns uncertain
obligations into a three-way sort: present obligation from a past event + probable
outflow + reliable estimate = provision, at undiscounted best estimate (except
discounted decommissioning-type liabilities capitalised into PPE); possible or
not-probable obligations are disclosed as contingent liabilities unless remote;
contingent assets stay out of the books and the notes, surfacing only in the
approving authority's report until virtually certain. No provisions for future
operating losses; restructuring only against binding commitments and only for direct
costs; reimbursements as separate assets when virtually certain; provisions reviewed,
reversed when stale, and spent only on what they were raised for.
