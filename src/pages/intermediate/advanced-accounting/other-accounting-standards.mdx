Two standards share this chapter, and they could hardly be less alike. AS 12 answers a
question every subsidised enterprise faces: **when a government hands you money (or
land), is that income — and if so, whose year is it?** Its machinery is small — two
recognition conditions, two presentation methods for asset grants, a special berth for
promoters'-contribution grants, and a refund rule for each — but the exam mines every
corner of it. AS 14 governs **amalgamations in the books of the transferee**; its full
working mechanics — merger vs purchase, purchase consideration, pooling and purchase
entries, statutory reserves — already have a chapter of their own
([Ch 13 · Amalgamation of Companies](/intermediate/advanced-accounting/amalgamation-of-companies/)),
so here we cover **the standard's own text**: what it scopes out, contingent
consideration, the goodwill amortisation factors, the disclosure lists, and what
happens when the amalgamation lands after the balance sheet date.

Applicability shorthand (regime in
[Ch 3](/intermediate/advanced-accounting/applicability-of-accounting-standards/)):
**AS 12 binds every entity in full** — it appears on no SMC or MSME relief list.
AS 14 is treated as **not applicable to MSMEs** only because amalgamations rarely
occur there; if one does occur, the standard applies.

## 1. AS 12: Accounting for Government Grants

### What is a government grant

Government grants are **assistance by government in cash or kind** for **past or
future compliance with certain conditions** — subsidies, cash incentives, duty
drawbacks. Two things fail the definition: assistance that **cannot reasonably be
valued** (free technical advice, guarantees), and transactions **indistinguishable
from normal trading** (government as a customer). The standard also leaves out three
neighbouring problems: grant accounting under changing-price regimes, government
assistance other than grants, and **government participation in the ownership** of
the enterprise.

### Recognition: two assurances, and receipt proves neither

Recognise a grant only when there is **reasonable assurance** that:

1. the enterprise **will comply with the conditions** attached to it; **and**
2. the grant **will be received**.

Mere receipt is **not conclusive evidence** that the conditions have been or will be
fulfilled — cash in the bank with doubtful compliance is still no recognition.

### The two approaches

The **capital approach** treats a grant as part of shareholders' funds; the **income
approach** takes it to income over one or more periods. AS 12 splits by nature:
grants with the character of **promoters' contribution** follow the capital approach;
everything else follows the income approach — matched against the related costs.

### Grants related to specific fixed assets

These are grants whose **primary condition** is that the qualifying enterprise
should **purchase, construct or otherwise acquire** the asset. Two presentations are
acceptable:

- **Method I — deduct from the asset.** The grant comes off the gross value; the
  P&L feels it as a **reduced depreciation charge** over the life. Where the grant
  equals the whole (or virtually the whole) cost, the asset sits in the balance
  sheet at a **nominal value**.
- **Method II — deferred income.** The grant is credited to **Deferred Government
  Grant** and released to the P&L on a **systematic and rational basis over the
  useful life** — in practice, in the proportions in which depreciation is charged.
  Grants against **non-depreciable** assets go to **capital reserve** instead
  (unless attached obligations make income recognition over the obligation period
  appropriate).

**Worked example 1 — one asset, both methods.** Plant costs ₹25,00,000 (life 5
years, no salvage); grant ₹5,00,000. *Method I*: asset recorded at 20,00,000;
depreciation ₹4,00,000 a year — the grant surfaces as ₹1,00,000 of depreciation
saved annually. *Method II*: asset at 25,00,000, depreciation ₹5,00,000, and
₹1,00,000 of deferred grant credited to the P&L each year. Same net charge
(₹4,00,000) either way — the difference is presentation.

### Grants related to revenue

Presented either **as a credit in the P&L** — separately or under "Other Income" —
or **deducted from the related expense**. Both are acceptable; a multi-year support
grant is spread on a straight-line basis over the years it compensates.

A grant that compensates **expenses or losses already incurred**, or gives
**immediate financial support with no further related costs**, is recognised in the
P&L **in the period it becomes receivable** — as an **extraordinary item where
appropriate (AS 5)**. There is nothing to defer: the cost it matches is already
behind you.

### Grants in the nature of promoters' contribution

Given **with reference to the total investment or capital outlay** (a central
investment subsidy), with **no repayment ordinarily expected**: credit to **capital
reserve** — **neither distributable as dividend nor deferred income**. Recognising
such a grant in the P&L is wrong precisely because it is not earned — it is an
incentive without related costs.

<Callout kind="mistake">
A grant received for a specific purpose cannot be routed elsewhere — a factory-setup
grant spent partly as dividend violates AS 12. And a non-monetary grant received
free is not "nothing happened": record the asset at a **nominal value**. Land at a
concessional price is recorded at its **acquisition cost**, not fair value.
</Callout>

### Refunds: undo the original treatment, prospectively

A grant that becomes refundable (conditions breached) is treated as an
**extraordinary item (AS 5)**. The unwinding tracks how the grant first went in:

- **Revenue grant** — apply the refund **first against any unamortised deferred
  credit**; the excess (or the whole amount, if nothing is deferred) hits the
  **P&L immediately**.
- **Asset grant** — either **increase the book value** of the asset or reduce the
  deferred income balance. Where the book value goes up, depreciation on the
  **revised** book value runs **prospectively over the residual life** — never a
  retrospective rework of past depreciation.
- **Promoters'-contribution grant** — the refundable amount is **reduced from
  capital reserve**.

**Worked example 2 — refund under the deduction method.** Machine ₹30,00,000, grant
₹6,00,000, residual value ₹6,00,000, life 4 years, SLM, asset shown net. Year 1:
asset 24,00,000, depreciation (24 − 6)/4 = ₹4,50,000, book value 19,50,000. Early in
year 2 the grant becomes refundable to the extent of ₹4,00,000: book value rises to
23,50,000, and depreciation for each remaining year is (23,50,000 − 6,00,000)/3 =
**₹5,83,333** — prospective only; year 1's charge stays untouched.

**Worked example 3 — refund of a revenue grant.** A ₹60,00,000 grant supports salary
costs over 4 years — ₹15,00,000 credited to the P&L annually from the deferred
account. At the start of year 3 the whole grant becomes refundable: the remaining
deferred credit of ₹30,00,000 absorbs the first half of the repayment and the other
**₹30,00,000 is debited straight to the P&L** as an extraordinary item.

### Disclosure

Two items: the **accounting policy** adopted (including presentation methods), and
the **nature and extent** of grants recognised — expressly including **non-monetary
grants at concessional rates or free of cost**.

## 2. AS 14: Accounting for Amalgamations

The full mechanics — merger vs purchase conditions, purchase consideration by net
payment and net assets, pooling and purchase entries, statutory reserves and the
Amalgamation Adjustment Reserve, closing the transferor's books — live in
[Ch 13](/intermediate/advanced-accounting/amalgamation-of-companies/) and are not
repeated here. What this unit adds is the standard's own frame:

**Scope.** AS 14 governs the **transferee company's books** in an amalgamation
pursuant to the Companies Act 2013 or another applicable statute — the **transferor
dissolves**. It does **not deal with acquisitions**: the distinguishing feature of an
acquisition is that the **acquired company is not dissolved and its separate entity
continues to exist**. *Transferor* = the company amalgamated into another;
*transferee* = the company it merges into.

**Consideration and later adjustments.** Consideration is the aggregate of shares,
other securities and payments (cash or other assets) made **to the shareholders of
the transferor** — measured at fair value. Where a scheme provides for adjustment
in the light of future events: include the additional payment in consideration at
the date of amalgamation **if it is probable and can reasonably be estimated**;
otherwise, recognise it **as soon as the amount is determinable**.

**Goodwill on amalgamation.** Amortise over a period **not exceeding five years**
unless a somewhat longer period can be justified. Estimating useful life weighs: the
foreseeable life of the business or industry; product obsolescence and demand
changes; the service-life expectancies of key individuals or groups; expected
competitor actions; and legal, regulatory or contractual provisions.

**The P&L balance.** In a **merger**, the transferor's Profit and Loss Account
balance is **aggregated** with the transferee's (or transferred to General
Reserve). In a **purchase**, it **loses its identity** — debit or credit.

**Amalgamation after the balance sheet date.** Effected after the year end but
before the financial statements are issued → **disclose under AS 4**; do **not**
incorporate it into the year's statements. It may still inform the year's numbers —
for instance, where the scheme is what lets the **going concern** assumption stand.

**Disclosures — three lists.** For **all** amalgamations, in the first financial
statements after it: names and general nature of the amalgamating businesses,
effective date for accounting, the method used, and particulars of the sanctioned
scheme. **Pooling adds**: description and number of shares issued with the
**percentage of each company's equity shares exchanged**, and the difference between
consideration and net identifiable assets with its treatment. **Purchase adds**: the
consideration (including any **contingently payable** element) and the
consideration/net-assets difference with its treatment — **including the goodwill
amortisation period**.

<Callout kind="pointer">
Exam sorting rule: computation questions on amalgamation belong to Ch 13's toolkit;
this unit's marks come from the standard's text — scope (acquisitions stay out),
the five-year goodwill presumption and its factors, the three disclosure lists,
contingent consideration, and the AS 4 route for post-year-end schemes.
</Callout>

## 3. The chapter in one paragraph

AS 12 recognises a government grant only on twin reasonable assurances — condition
compliance and receipt, with receipt itself proving nothing — then presents it by
nature: asset grants either shrink the asset (reduced depreciation) or sit as
deferred income released over the life; revenue grants credit the P&L or net against
the expense; past-cost compensation lands in the P&L when receivable; and
promoters'-contribution grants rest in capital reserve, unavailable for dividend.
Refunds are extraordinary items that reverse the original route — deferred credit
first for revenue grants, book-value increase with prospective depreciation for
asset grants, capital-reserve reduction for promoters' grants. AS 14, beyond the
Ch 13 mechanics, scopes out acquisitions (no dissolution), includes contingent
consideration when probable and estimable, amortises amalgamation goodwill within
five years on listed factors, aggregates the P&L balance in a merger but erases it
in a purchase, sends post-balance-sheet-date schemes to AS 4 disclosure, and
prescribes disclosure lists for all amalgamations plus method-specific extras.
