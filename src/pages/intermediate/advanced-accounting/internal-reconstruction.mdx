A company that has bled losses for years ends up with a balance sheet that lies politely:
assets carried above what they could ever earn, a fat debit balance in the P&L, goodwill and
preliminary expenses that represent nothing. Internal reconstruction is the surgery that makes
the books honest again — writing the dead weight off against capital that shareholders (and
sometimes creditors) agree to sacrifice — **without killing the company or forming a new one**.
That last clause is the boundary with Ch 13: external reconstruction builds a new company and
accounts under AS 14; internal reconstruction reorganises the same legal entity from inside.

For the exam this chapter is one dependable pattern: build the **Capital Reduction (or
Reconstruction) Account**, pour every sacrifice into its credit side, write off every hollow
asset from its debit side, and sweep the balance to capital reserve. Everything else is the
statutory frame around that account.

## 1. What internal reconstruction is

**Internal reconstruction** is the reorganisation of a company's capital structure — revaluing
assets, reassessing liabilities, and writing off accumulated losses — through a write-down of
the shares' paid-up value and/or a variation of class rights, all within the existing company.

The tell-tale patient: continuous losses, a P&L debit balance carried forward, fictitious
assets (preliminary expenses, discount on issue), unrepresented intangibles, and overvalued
fixed assets. The company is over-capitalised: its stated capital is not represented by real
assets. The cure is to admit the loss and make someone absorb it — usually the equity
shareholders first, as the residual risk-takers.

Contrast the two reconstructions:

| | Internal reconstruction | External reconstruction |
|---|---|---|
| Entity | Same company survives | New company formed; old one wound up |
| Governing law | ss. 61, 66 (and schemes) of the Companies Act 2013 | AS 14 accounting; s.232 machinery |
| Losses | Written off against capital sacrifices via the Capital Reduction A/c | Left behind in the dying company |
| Accounting | Capital Reduction / Reconstruction Account | Realisation A/c (transferor) + purchase-method entries (transferee) |

## 2. Alteration of share capital (s.61)

A limited company may alter its capital clause **if the articles authorise it, by an ordinary
resolution** in general meeting — no tribunal involved. Four alterations matter here:

1. **Increase** of authorised share capital.
2. **Consolidation and sub-division** — repackaging shares into larger or smaller
   denominations.
3. **Conversion** of fully paid shares into stock, or stock back into shares.
4. **Cancellation of unissued shares** — shares not taken or agreed to be taken by anyone.
   This trims the *authorised* figure only; it touches nobody's money.

<Callout kind="pointer">
  <p>
    <strong>None of these opens a Capital Reduction Account.</strong> Alterations rearrange
    the packaging of capital; nobody sacrifices anything. The same is true of a variation of
    shareholders' rights (s.48). The Capital Reduction Account enters only with s.66
    reductions, compromises with outsiders, and surrender schemes — that mapping is a
    standing MCQ.
  </p>
</Callout>

### The proportion rule for sub-division

On a sub-division or consolidation, the **ratio between paid-up and unpaid amounts must stay
what it was**.

### Worked Example 1 — sub-division arithmetic

Tapti Sugars Ltd has 20,000 equity shares of ₹50 each, ₹40 paid up. It sub-divides them into
shares of ₹10 each.

**Working.** Each ₹50 share becomes 5 shares of ₹10 → 20,000 × 5 = **1,00,000 shares**. Paid-up
proportion was 40/50 = 80%, so each new ₹10 share carries **₹8 paid up** (unpaid ₹2). Total
paid-up capital is unchanged: 1,00,000 × 8 = ₹8,00,000 = 20,000 × 40. No journal beyond
closing the old capital account into the new — and no Capital Reduction Account.

## 3. Reduction of share capital (s.66)

Reduction is the real surgery: paid-up money (or the obligation to pay it) is extinguished.
Because creditors' cushion shrinks, s.66 demands a **special resolution** confirmed by the
**Tribunal (NCLT)**. Three statutory modes:

- **(a) Extinguish or reduce the liability for unpaid amounts.** ₹10 shares, ₹7.50 paid:
  cancel the uncalled ₹2.50 and the shares become ₹7.50 fully paid. Nobody pays or receives
  anything now; the *future* call disappears. Entry: `Share Capital (₹10 partly paid) Dr →
  To Share Capital (₹7.50 fully paid)` — **no Capital Reduction credit arises**, because no
  paid-up money was sacrificed.
- **(b) Pay off capital in excess of needs.** A cash-rich company returns, say, ₹25 per ₹100
  share: capital falls and cash leaves. This is a genuine reduction but produces no
  reconstruction credit either — value went back to the shareholders.
- **(c) Cancel paid-up capital that is lost** or unrepresented by available assets — the
  reconstruction workhorse. The amount shareholders give up is credited to the **Capital
  Reduction Account** (also called the Reconstruction Account).

The rupee mechanics of mode (c), for a ₹100 share written down to ₹10:

| Variant | Entry |
|---|---|
| Paid-up value reduced, face value retained | `Share Capital A/c Dr ₹90 → To Capital Reduction A/c ₹90` (per share) |
| Both face and paid-up value reduced | `Share Capital (₹100) A/c Dr ₹100 → To Share Capital (₹10) A/c ₹10 → To Capital Reduction A/c ₹90` (per share) |

<Callout kind="mistake">
  <p>
    <strong>Mode (a) creates NO reconstruction credit.</strong> Cancelling uncalled capital
    swaps a partly paid share for a smaller fully paid one — no reserves are freed, nothing
    is available to write losses against. Only mode (c) — cancelling PAID-UP capital — feeds
    the Capital Reduction Account. Stems love asking "how much is credited to Capital
    Reduction?" after a mode-(a) cancellation; the answer is nil.
  </p>
</Callout>

## 4. Compromise, arrangement and surrender

Shareholders are not the only ones who can absorb losses:

- **Equity shareholders give up reserves** standing in the books:
  `Reserves A/c Dr → To Reconstruction A/c`.
- **Outside liabilities settle for less.** Creditors owed ₹4,80,000 accepting 75 paise in the
  rupee sacrifice ₹1,20,000: `Trade Creditors A/c Dr (sacrifice) → To Reconstruction A/c`.
  Debenture holders exchanging old debentures for a smaller amount of new ones do the same.
- **Surrender of shares.** The scheme sub-divides shares into small denominations, has
  shareholders surrender a slice of them to the company, and re-allots the surrendered
  shares to debenture holders/creditors in discharge of their claims. Whatever surrendered
  shares remain **unutilised are cancelled** — credited to the Reconstruction Account.

### Worked Example 2 — a surrender scheme

Krishna Textiles Ltd has 40,000 shares of ₹25 fully paid. The scheme: sub-divide into ₹5
shares (2,00,000 shares); shareholders surrender 40% of their holdings; debenture holders
owed ₹3,00,000 accept 56,000 of the surrendered shares in full settlement.

**Working Note 1 — surrender.** Shares surrendered = 40% × 2,00,000 = 80,000 shares =
₹4,00,000 of capital, held in trust for the scheme.

**Working Note 2 — debenture settlement.** 56,000 shares (₹2,80,000) discharge a ₹3,00,000
claim → debenture holders' sacrifice = **₹20,000** to the Reconstruction Account.

**Working Note 3 — cancellation.** Unutilised surrendered shares = 80,000 − 56,000 = 24,000
shares = **₹1,20,000**, cancelled and credited to the Reconstruction Account.

## 5. The Capital Reduction Account at work

Assemble the account in a fixed order:

**Credit side (the sacrifices):** capital cancelled under mode (c); reserves given up by
shareholders; sacrifices by creditors and debenture holders; **appreciation on revaluation
of any asset** (debit the asset, credit Capital Reduction).

**Debit side (the write-offs):** the P&L debit balance; goodwill and other worthless
intangibles; fictitious assets (preliminary expenses, discount on issue); the overvalued
slice of fixed assets and inventory; and the **expenses of the reconstruction scheme**
itself.

**The balance:** a credit left over goes to **Capital Reserve**. If the write-offs exceed the
sacrifices, existing reserves in the books are drawn in to cover the gap.

### Worked Example 3 — the full account

Godavari Mills Ltd (and Reduced) implements: equity capital ₹10,00,000 (1,00,000 shares of
₹10) written down to ₹6 per share (face value also ₹6); freehold land appreciated by
₹80,000; creditors of ₹1,50,000 accept 90% in settlement; write off P&L debit balance
₹3,90,000 and goodwill ₹70,000; scheme expenses ₹5,000.

**Working Note 1 — credits.** Capital: 1,00,000 × (10 − 6) = ₹4,00,000. Land ₹80,000.
Creditors 10% × 1,50,000 = ₹15,000. Total credits = **₹4,95,000**.

**Working Note 2 — debits.** 3,90,000 + 70,000 + 5,000 = **₹4,65,000**.

**Working Note 3 — balance.** 4,95,000 − 4,65,000 = **₹30,000 to Capital Reserve**. New
paid-up capital: 1,00,000 × 6 = ₹6,00,000.

<Callout kind="example" label="Working-note discipline = marks">
  <p>
    Structure every answer as: (1) capital sacrifice per class, (2) outsiders' sacrifices,
    (3) revaluation credits, (4) write-off list, (5) the account itself with the balance to
    capital reserve, (6) the post-scheme balance sheet. Examiners award the account and the
    balance sheet separately — an arithmetic slip in one need not sink the other if the
    working notes are labelled.
  </p>
</Callout>

## 6. The reconstructed balance sheet

Two presentation rules with statutory teeth:

- **"and Reduced":** the company adds these words after its name **only if the Tribunal so
  orders** — it is not automatic.
- **Five-year disclosure:** the amount written off fixed assets under the scheme must be
  **shown for five years** in the accounts, so readers can see the surgery scar while it
  heals.

Beyond that, the balance sheet is ordinary Schedule III: the new (smaller) share capital,
the capital reserve from the scheme's surplus, assets at their written-down honest values —
and, if the scheme did its job, no accumulated-loss line at all.

That's the chapter. Sort every scheme instruction into one of four buckets — alteration
(§2, no reduction account), reduction (§3), outsider sacrifice or surrender (§4) — then run
the Capital Reduction Account (§5) and present the result (§6).
