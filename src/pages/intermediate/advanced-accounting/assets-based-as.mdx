Everything on the asset side of the balance sheet gets its rulebook in this chapter.
Seven standards answer the same three questions for seven kinds of asset: **what goes
into cost**, **what the carrying amount may not exceed**, and **when value lost must be
recognised**. Inventory may not sit above net realisable value (AS 2); PPE carries the
full cost of getting it ready and may be revalued by class (AS 10); investments split by
intention into current and long-term with different ceilings (AS 13); borrowing costs
attach to assets that take substantial time to build (AS 16); leases follow economic
substance, not legal title (AS 19); intangibles must earn recognition and amortise
against a ten-year presumption (AS 26); and when any of it stops earning its keep, AS 28
writes it down to recoverable amount. This is the most numerical chapter of the AS
clusters — every unit has a computation the exam can set.

Applicability shorthand (the regime lives in
[Ch 3](/intermediate/advanced-accounting/applicability-of-accounting-standards/)):
AS 2, 10, 13 and 16 bind everyone in full. AS 19 and AS 28 carry **disclosure and
measurement relaxations** for SMCs and MSMEs — most usefully, the smaller entities may
estimate value in use as a **reasonable estimate** instead of running a present-value
computation — and AS 28 is off entirely for the smaller MSME sub-class.

## 1. AS 2: Valuation of Inventories

Inventories are assets held **for sale in the ordinary course of business** (finished
goods), **in the process of production** for such sale (WIP), or **as materials or
supplies to be consumed** in production or rendering of services. Four things sit
outside AS 2: WIP under construction contracts (AS 7 territory, including cement lying
at a construction site), WIP of service providers, shares/debentures held as
stock-in-trade, and producers' inventories of livestock, agricultural, forest and
mineral products carried at NRV by established industry practice.

The master rule: value inventories at the **lower of cost and net realisable value**,
applied **item by item** (or by groups of similar items) — never on a global basis.

**Cost** has three layers:

- **Purchase** — price plus duties and taxes (excluding those **recoverable** from the
  authorities), freight inwards and other directly attributable acquisition costs, less
  trade discounts and rebates.
- **Conversion** — direct labour plus production overheads. **Variable** overheads ride
  on actual production; **fixed** overheads are absorbed at **normal capacity** — the
  average production expected over periods under normal circumstances, allowing for
  planned maintenance. Actual output below normal → the unabsorbed fixed overhead is
  **expensed**, not loaded onto fewer units. Abnormally high output → the rate is cut to
  actual, so inventory never carries more than actual fixed overhead.
- **Other costs** — only to the extent incurred to bring inventory to its present
  location and condition.

**Excluded from cost and expensed when incurred**: abnormal waste of materials, labour
or production costs; storage costs (unless a necessary stage before further
production); administrative overheads that don't bring inventory to location/condition;
selling and distribution costs. Interest enters only where AS 16's conditions are met.

**Cost formulas**: where items are not ordinarily interchangeable (or are earmarked for
specific projects), use **specific identification**. Otherwise, **FIFO or weighted
average** — whichever gives the fairest approximation of the cost actually incurred.
LIFO is not an option. **Standard cost** and the **retail method** are acceptable
measurement *techniques* if they approximate actual cost.

**Net realisable value** = estimated selling price in the ordinary course of business −
estimated costs of completion − estimated costs necessary to make the sale. Estimates
rest on the most reliable evidence at the time of estimation, adjusted for
post-balance-sheet events that confirm year-end conditions.

<Callout kind="pointer">
**Materials held for use** are NOT written below cost so long as the finished product
they will enter is expected to sell **at or above cost**. Only when the finished
product itself falls below cost are the materials written down — and then their
**replacement cost** may be the best available measure of NRV.
</Callout>

Joint products: allocate joint costs on a rational and consistent basis (relative sales
value at split-off is typical). **By-products, scrap and waste** are measured at NRV,
which is **deducted from the cost of the main product**. Disclose the accounting policy
including the cost formula used, and the total carrying amount with its classification.

<Callout kind="example">
Fixed production overheads ₹3,00,000; normal capacity 12,000 units; actual production
10,000 units. Absorption rate = ₹25 per unit (never ₹30): inventory absorbs
₹2,50,000 and the ₹50,000 of unabsorbed overhead is expensed in the P&L.
</Callout>

## 2. AS 10: Property, Plant and Equipment

PPE are **tangible** items **held for use** in production or supply of goods or
services, for rental to others, or for administrative purposes, and **expected to be
used during more than twelve months**. Recognition needs the usual pair: probable
future economic benefits and reliably measurable cost. **Bearer plants** are PPE; the
**produce on them is not**. **Investment property** (defined in AS 13) is accounted for
only under the **cost model** of this standard.

**Cost** = purchase price (including import duties and non-refundable taxes, net of
trade discounts and rebates) **+ directly attributable costs** of bringing the asset to
the location and condition for intended operation (site preparation, delivery and
handling, installation and assembly, professional fees, testing costs) **+ the initial
estimate of decommissioning, restoration and similar obligations** incurred on
acquisition. **Never cost**: expenses of opening a new facility, advertising and
promotion, administration and general overheads, **initial operating losses**, and
relocation/reorganisation costs. Capitalisation stops when the asset is in the location
and condition to operate as intended — costs of the period while it waits to be used,
or runs below capacity, are expensed.

Self-constructed assets follow the same principles: internal profits are eliminated and
the cost of **abnormal** wasted material, labour or other resources stays out.
**Exchanges of assets** are measured at **fair value** unless the exchange lacks
commercial substance or neither fair value is reliably measurable — then use the
carrying amount of the asset given up. **Spare parts, stand-by and servicing
equipment** are PPE when they meet the definition; otherwise they are inventory.
Replacement parts and **regular major inspections** are capitalised into the carrying
amount when the recognition criteria are met, with the replaced part or previous
inspection derecognised.

After recognition, choose per **entire class**: the **cost model** (cost less
accumulated depreciation and impairment) or the **revaluation model** (fair value at
revaluation date less subsequent depreciation/impairment), with revaluations regular
enough that carrying amounts stay near fair value. The direction rules:

- **Increase** → credit **revaluation surplus** in owners' interests; except to the
  extent it reverses a previous decrease of the same asset charged to P&L → credit P&L.
- **Decrease** → charge to P&L; except to the extent a credit balance exists in the
  revaluation surplus for that asset → debit the surplus.
- The surplus may be transferred to **revenue reserves** when the asset is
  derecognised — or progressively, as the asset is used, by the difference between
  depreciation on the revalued amount and on original cost. Never through the P&L.

**Depreciation**: each part with a significant cost and a different useful life is
depreciated **separately** (component approach). Land has an unlimited life and is not
depreciated (quarries and landfills aside). Residual value, useful life and method are
reviewed at least each year-end; a change in any of them — including a **change of
depreciation method** — is a **change in accounting estimate**, applied prospectively
with AS 5 disclosure. Items **retired from active use and held for disposal** are
carried at the **lower of carrying amount and net realisable value**. On derecognition,
the gain or loss (proceeds − carrying amount) goes to the P&L — and gains are **not
classified as revenue**.

## 3. AS 13: Accounting for Investments

Investments are assets held for **income, appreciation or other benefits** — not
stock-in-trade (securities held for sale in the ordinary course of business are
outside AS 13). The split that drives everything: a **current investment** is by nature
**readily realisable and intended to be held for not more than one year** from
acquisition; everything else is **long-term**.

**Cost** includes brokerage, fees and duties. Securities issued as consideration →
cost is the **fair value of the securities issued**; asset exchanged → fair value of
the asset given up (or of the investment received if more clearly evident).
**Pre-acquisition dividends** and interest for pre-acquisition periods are a **recovery
of cost**, not income. **Rights shares subscribed** → add the subscription cost to the
carrying amount of the holding. Rights **sold** without subscribing → proceeds go to
the **P&L** — except where the original holding was bought **cum-right** and the market
value ex-right has fallen below cost, in which case applying the proceeds to reduce the
carrying amount is appropriate.

Carrying amounts:

- **Current** — **lower of cost and fair value**, determined **individually or by
  category**, but never on an overall (global) basis.
- **Long-term** — **cost**, reduced (individually, investment by investment) for any
  decline **other than temporary**; the reduction is charged to the P&L and **reversed**
  when the decline no longer exists.

**Investment property** — land or buildings held for rental income or appreciation
rather than for use in operations — is a long-term investment carried under AS 10's
cost model. **Reclassification**: long-term → current, transfer at the **lower of cost
and carrying amount** at the transfer date; current → long-term, transfer at the
**lower of cost and fair value** at that date. On disposal, the difference between net
proceeds and carrying amount hits the P&L. Disclosures include gross income from
investments, profits/losses on disposals, and the **aggregate market value of quoted
investments** alongside the aggregate carrying amounts.

## 4. AS 16: Borrowing Costs

Borrowing costs are interest and other costs incurred **in connection with borrowing**:
interest and commitment charges, amortisation of discounts/premiums and of ancillary
issue costs, finance charges under AS 19 finance leases, and **exchange differences on
foreign-currency borrowings to the extent they are regarded as an adjustment to
interest costs** (capped at the interest a comparable rupee borrowing would have
carried). Owners' equity — including preference capital not classified as a liability —
is not a borrowing.

The rule: borrowing costs **directly attributable to the acquisition, construction or
production of a qualifying asset** are capitalised as part of its cost; all other
borrowing costs are expensed. A **qualifying asset** necessarily takes a **substantial
period of time** — ordinarily **twelve months** — to get ready for intended use or sale
(manufacturing plants, power facilities, long-gestation inventories, investment
properties). Assets ready for use on acquisition, and inventories routinely produced in
large quantities on a repetitive basis, don't qualify.

The arithmetic splits by funding source:

- **Specific borrowings** → capitalise **actual borrowing costs incurred less income on
  the temporary investment** of the unspent funds.
- **General borrowings** → apply a **capitalisation rate** (the weighted average cost
  of general borrowings outstanding) to the expenditure on the asset — capped at the
  total borrowing costs actually incurred in the period.

<Callout kind="example">
Specific 12% loan of ₹40,00,000 for a plant, idle funds earning ₹60,000: capitalise
4,80,000 − 60,000 = ₹4,20,000. General pool: ₹25,00,000 at 10% and ₹25,00,000 at 14% →
capitalisation rate (2,50,000 + 3,50,000)/50,00,000 = 12%, applied to the qualifying
expenditure funded from the pool.
</Callout>

Timing: **commencement** needs all three — expenditure on the asset is being incurred,
borrowing costs are being incurred, and activities necessary to prepare the asset are
in progress (including technical/administrative work before construction).
Capitalisation is **suspended** while active development stands interrupted for an
**extended period** — but not for temporary delays that are necessary parts of the
process or inherent to the asset (a bridge waiting out the monsoon keeps capitalising
where that interruption is usual in the territory). **Cessation** when substantially all the activities necessary
to prepare the asset are complete; a part that can be used while construction continues
on other parts stops capitalising when that part is ready. If the resulting carrying
amount exceeds recoverable amount, write it down per the other standards. Disclose the
policy and the **amount of borrowing costs capitalised during the period**.

## 5. AS 19: Leases

A lease transfers the **right to use** an asset for an agreed period in return for
payments — and AS 19 accounts for the economics, not the paperwork. Outside its scope:
lease agreements to explore for or use natural resources, licensing agreements for
items like films, video recordings, plays, patents and copyrights, and **leases of
land**.

A **finance lease** transfers **substantially all the risks and rewards incident to
ownership** — title may or may not eventually pass. The classic situations, any of
which normally points to a finance lease: ownership transfers by the end of the term; a
**bargain purchase option**; the term covers the **major part of the asset's economic
life** even without title transfer; the **present value of the minimum lease payments
is at least substantially all of the asset's fair value** at inception; the asset is of
a **specialised nature** usable only by the lessee without major modification.
Supporting indicators: lessee bears the lessor's cancellation losses, residual-value
fluctuations fall on the lessee, or a bargain-rent renewal exists. Everything else is
an **operating lease**.

**Lessee, finance lease**: recognise an asset and a liability at the **lower of the
fair value of the leased asset and the present value of the minimum lease payments**
(discounted at the interest rate implicit in the lease, or failing that the incremental
borrowing rate). Each payment splits into a **finance charge** (rate on the outstanding
liability) and a **principal reduction**. Depreciate per AS 10 consistently with owned
assets — and if there is **no reasonable certainty of ownership** at the end, over the
**shorter of the lease term and useful life**.

**Lessee, operating lease**: rent is expensed on a **straight-line basis** over the
lease term unless another systematic basis better represents the time pattern of the
benefit — deliberately ignoring stepped or back-loaded cash payments.

**Lessor, finance lease**: derecognise the asset and recognise a **receivable equal to
the net investment** in the lease. Gross investment = minimum lease payments (from the
lessor's standpoint) + **unguaranteed residual value**; **unearned finance income =
gross investment − present value** of those components at the implicit rate; income is
recognised at a **constant periodic rate of return on the net investment outstanding**.
**Lessor, operating lease**: the asset stays on the lessor's balance sheet, is
depreciated per AS 10, and rental income is recognised straight-line unless another
basis fits better.

**Sale and leaseback**: if the leaseback is a **finance lease**, any excess of sale
proceeds over carrying amount is **deferred and amortised over the lease term in
proportion to depreciation** — the "sale" is in substance a loan on the asset's
security. If the leaseback is an **operating lease**, the result depends on fair value:
at fair value, recognise profit or loss immediately; below fair value, recognise
immediately **unless** the loss is compensated by below-market future rentals (defer
and amortise in proportion to the rentals); above fair value, the excess over fair
value is deferred and amortised over the expected period of use.

<Callout kind="mistake">
The lessee's finance-lease asset is NOT recorded at fair value by default — it is the
**lower** of fair value and PV of minimum lease payments. And the depreciation life is
the **shorter** of lease term and useful life only when ownership is not reasonably
certain; with reasonable certainty, use the useful life, full stop.
</Callout>

## 6. AS 26: Intangible Assets

An intangible asset is an **identifiable, non-monetary asset without physical
substance**, held for use in production/supply, rental, or administration. Recognition
demands the definition — identifiability (separable from goodwill), **control** (power
to obtain the benefits and restrict others' access, usually via legal rights), future
economic benefits — plus the standard pair: probable benefits and reliably measurable
cost. AS 26 also governs expenditure on **advertising, training, start-up and R&D
activities**, and rights under licensing agreements for films, recordings, plays,
manuscripts, patents and copyrights.

The famous prohibitions: **internally generated goodwill is never recognised** — it is
not an identifiable resource controlled at measurable cost. Internally generated
**brands, mastheads, publishing titles and customer lists** are likewise never
recognised. Expenditure once expensed is **never reinstated** as an asset later.

Internal projects split into two phases. **Research** (original, planned investigation
for new knowledge) is **always expensed**. **Development** expenditure is capitalised
**if and only if all six** are demonstrated:

1. **Technical feasibility** of completing the asset for use or sale;
2. **Intention** to complete and use or sell it;
3. **Ability** to use or sell it;
4. **How the asset will generate probable future benefits** (a market exists for its
   output or the asset itself, or it is internally useful);
5. **Adequate resources** — technical, financial and other — to complete it;
6. **Reliable measurement** of the attributable expenditure.

If the research phase cannot be distinguished from the development phase, treat the
whole project **as research** — expense it. Expenditure on advertising, training and
start-up activities is expensed when incurred.

After recognition: **cost less accumulated amortisation and impairment losses** — AS 26
offers **no revaluation model**. Amortise over the **best estimate of useful life**,
with a **rebuttable presumption that useful life will not exceed ten years** from the
date the asset is available for use. A life from legal rights cannot exceed the legal
term unless renewal is **virtually certain**. The method mirrors the consumption
pattern (straight-line if it can't be determined reliably); the **residual value is
assumed to be zero** unless a third party has committed to buy the asset at the end of
its life or an active market exists that will survive the asset's useful life. Review
the amortisation period and method at least each year end.

## 7. AS 28: Impairment of Assets

Depreciation spreads cost; **impairment recognises that the cost will not come back**.
At each balance sheet date the enterprise checks for **indications** of impairment —
external (a material fall in market value beyond normal use/time effects; adverse
changes in the technological, market, economic or legal environment; market interest
rates rising enough to bite the discount rate; **carrying amount of net assets
exceeding market capitalisation**) and internal (obsolescence or physical damage;
restructuring, idle status or plans to dispose; internal evidence of
worse-than-expected performance). Only if an indication exists must recoverable amount
be estimated.

**Recoverable amount = the higher of net selling price and value in use.**

- **Net selling price** — the amount obtainable in an arm's-length sale between
  knowledgeable, willing parties, less costs of disposal.
- **Value in use** — the **present value** of estimated future cash flows from
  continuing use plus the residual on ultimate disposal. Projections rest on reasonable
  and supportable assumptions, on budgets/forecasts of at most **five years**, with
  steady-or-declining growth extrapolation beyond (not above the long-term average for
  the products, industries or countries involved). The **discount rate is pre-tax**,
  reflecting current market assessments of the time value of money and the risks
  specific to the asset.

If recoverable amount is below carrying amount, write the asset down and take the
**impairment loss to the P&L** (revalued assets: against the revaluation surplus
first). Depreciation for future periods is then re-planned on the new carrying amount
over the remaining life.

Where an individual asset generates no largely independent cash inflows, test its
**cash-generating unit** — the smallest identifiable group of assets whose cash inflows
are largely independent of those from other assets. Goodwill rides with the CGUs it
belongs to: apply the **bottom-up test** (allocate goodwill to the unit where a
reasonable, consistent basis exists and test the unit with it); where goodwill cannot
be so allocated, add the **top-down test** at the smallest unit that carries it. An
impairment loss for a CGU is allocated in strict order: **first to goodwill**, then to
the other assets **pro-rata to their carrying amounts** — but no asset falls below the
highest of its own net selling price, value in use and zero (the excess re-allocates).

<Callout kind="example">
CGU: goodwill ₹1,00,000, PPE ₹6,00,000, intangibles ₹2,00,000 (total ₹9,00,000);
recoverable amount ₹6,75,000. Loss ₹2,25,000 → goodwill absorbs its full ₹1,00,000
first; the remaining ₹1,25,000 splits 6:2 over PPE and intangibles → ₹93,750 and
₹31,250.
</Callout>

**Reversals** mirror the recognition test: an indication that the loss has decreased →
re-estimate recoverable amount. The increased carrying amount may **not exceed the
carrying amount that would have stood (net of depreciation) had no impairment loss ever
been recognised** — anything above that line is revaluation, which belongs to AS 10.
Reversals go to the P&L (revalued assets: to the surplus), and depreciation is
re-planned again. A goodwill impairment is reversed only in the narrow case where the
loss was caused by a **specific external event of an exceptional nature** not expected
to recur, and subsequent external events have reversed its effect.

## 8. The chapter in one paragraph

AS 2 values inventory at the lower of cost (purchase + conversion with fixed overheads
at normal capacity + location/condition costs; FIFO or weighted average when specific
identification is impossible) and NRV, item by item, sparing raw materials while the
finished product clears cost. AS 10 loads PPE cost with everything needed to make the
asset operational including dismantling estimates, lets a whole class carry fair value
through the revaluation surplus, depreciates by component, and treats method changes as
estimates. AS 13 splits investments by intention — current at lower of cost and fair
value (never globally), long-term at cost less other-than-temporary declines — and
polices reclassification with lower-of rules. AS 16 capitalises borrowing costs on
qualifying assets: specific borrowings net of temporary investment income, general
borrowings at the weighted-average capitalisation rate, between commencement and
substantial completion, suspended in extended interruptions. AS 19 classifies by risks
and rewards: finance-lease lessees capitalise the lower of fair value and PV of minimum
lease payments and split each rental into interest and principal; lessors hold net
investment; sale-and-leaseback gains defer when the leaseback is financial. AS 26
expenses research, capitalises development only on six demonstrations, never recognises
internal goodwill or brands, and amortises against a rebuttable ten-year ceiling with
zero residual by default. AS 28 writes assets (or their cash-generating units) down to
the higher of net selling price and value in use — goodwill first, pro-rata next — and
caps any reversal at depreciated historical cost.
